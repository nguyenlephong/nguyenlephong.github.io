<h2 id="the-problem">The problem: services that hold hands</h2>
<p>When one service calls another and waits for the reply, we call it <strong>synchronous</strong>. It is the natural first instinct, because it reads like a normal function call. The trouble is what happens when you chain them.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Latency adds up.</strong> If each hop takes 80&nbsp;ms, a four-hop chain is 320&nbsp;ms before anyone does real work.</li>
<li><strong>Failure spreads.</strong> If the email service is down, checkout fails — even though emailing a receipt is not the point of buying something.</li>
<li><strong>Coupling hardens.</strong> The order service has to <em>know about</em> payment, inventory, and email. Add a fifth reaction and you edit the order service again.</li>
</ul>
<p>The deep issue is that the caller is doing two different jobs at once: <em>completing the order</em> and <em>orchestrating everyone who cares about the order</em>. Those are not the same job, and bolting them together is what makes the spinner spin.</p>

<h2 id="sync-vs-async">Two ways for services to talk</h2>
<p>There are exactly two shapes of conversation, and most architecture arguments are really arguments about which one to use where.</p>

<div class="blog-table-wrap">
<table>
  <thead>
    <tr><th width="22%">&nbsp;</th><th>Synchronous (request/response)</th><th>Asynchronous (messaging)</th></tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr><th>Mental model</th><td>A phone call — both sides on the line at once</td><td>A letter in the post — sent now, read later</td></tr>
    <tr><th>Coupling</th><td>Caller knows the callee and waits for it</td><td>Sender knows only the broker, not the readers</td></tr>
    <tr><th>Failure</th><td>Callee down &rarr; caller fails now</td><td>Reader down &rarr; message waits in the queue</td></tr>
    <tr><th>Latency</th><td>Sum of every hop</td><td>Sender returns immediately; work happens in the background</td></tr>
    <tr><th>Best for</th><td>"I need the answer to continue" (read a balance, validate a token)</td><td>"This happened; whoever cares can react" (order placed, file uploaded)</td></tr>
  </tbody>
</table>
</div>

<p>Notice the last row. Synchronous is the right tool when you genuinely <em>need the answer to keep going</em>. Asynchronous is the right tool when you are announcing that <em>something happened</em> and other people's reactions are their business, not yours. Event-driven architecture is what you get when you lean into that second shape on purpose.</p>
<p>Here is the same checkout, before and after. The synchronous version makes the customer wait for everyone:</p>
<pre><code><span class="cmt">// Before: checkout owns — and waits on — everybody downstream</span>
<span class="kw">async function</span> checkout(cart) {
  <span class="kw">const</span> order = <span class="kw">await</span> orders.create(cart)
  <span class="kw">await</span> payment.charge(order)        <span class="cmt">// slow</span>
  <span class="kw">await</span> inventory.reserve(order)      <span class="cmt">// slow</span>
  <span class="kw">await</span> email.sendReceipt(order)      <span class="cmt">// slow, and not the point</span>
  <span class="kw">return</span> order                        <span class="cmt">// customer waited for all four</span>
}</code></pre>
<p>The event-driven version finishes the order and <em>announces</em> it. Whoever cares reacts on their own time:</p>
<pre><code><span class="cmt">// After: checkout completes, then publishes a fact and returns</span>
<span class="kw">async function</span> checkout(cart) {
  <span class="kw">const</span> order = <span class="kw">await</span> orders.create(cart)
  <span class="kw">await</span> broker.publish(<span class="str">"orders"</span>, { type: <span class="str">"OrderPlaced"</span>, order })
  <span class="kw">return</span> order                        <span class="cmt">// fast — payment, inventory, email react later</span>
}</code></pre>
<p>The customer no longer pays the email service's slow afternoon. Just as importantly, adding fraud scoring tomorrow means writing a new subscriber, not editing <code>checkout</code> again.</p>

<h2 id="vocabulary">The vocabulary, in plain words</h2>
<p>The jargon is small once you strip the mystique off it.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Message</strong> — any blob of data one service hands to another through a middleman.</li>
<li><strong>Command</strong> — a message that <em>asks</em> for something to happen: <code>ChargeCard</code>. It has one intended handler and can be refused.</li>
<li><strong>Event</strong> — a message that <em>announces</em> something already happened: <code>OrderPlaced</code>. It is a fact in the past tense, addressed to no one in particular.</li>
<li><strong>Broker</strong> — the middleman that holds messages and delivers them: RabbitMQ, Kafka, NATS, AWS SNS/SQS, Google Pub/Sub.</li>
<li><strong>Topic</strong> / <strong>queue</strong> — the named mailbox a message lands in. Producers write to it; consumers read from it.</li>
<li><strong>Producer</strong> / <strong>consumer</strong> — the one who publishes, and the one who subscribes and reacts.</li>
</ul>
<p>The single most important distinction is <strong>command vs. event</strong>, because it decides who is in charge. A command is "<em>you, do this</em>." An event is "<em>this happened; do whatever you think is right</em>." Confuse the two and you build a system that looks event-driven but is secretly a pile of remote-control buttons.</p>

<h2 id="styles">Three flavours of "event"</h2>
<p>"We use events" can mean three quite different things. Knowing which one you mean saves a lot of arguments.</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Event notification.</strong> A thin ping: "order 1234 was placed." If a consumer needs details, it calls back to ask. Smallest payload, but it can create chatty call-backs.</li>
<li><strong>Event-carried state transfer.</strong> The event carries everything a consumer needs — the whole order — so no call-back is required. Heavier messages, but consumers stay independent. This is the workhorse of most event-driven systems.</li>
<li><strong>Event sourcing.</strong> The events <em>are</em> the source of truth; current state is rebuilt by replaying them. Powerful and rare — most teams do not need it, and we will treat it as an advanced option in the next part.</li>
</ol>
<div class="blog-callout blog-callout--tip"><span class="blog-callout__label">A simple default</span><p>Start with <strong>event-carried state transfer</strong> for the handful of facts other teams care about, and plain synchronous calls for everything that needs an immediate answer. You can live a long, happy life on just those two before you ever reach for event sourcing.</p></div>

<h2 id="delivery">What the broker actually promises</h2>
<p>Here is where good intentions meet physics. Brokers do not promise the clean "exactly once, in order" world you imagine. They promise something more honest and more awkward.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>At-least-once delivery is the norm.</strong> The broker may deliver the same message twice — after a network blip, a crash, or a redelivery. "Exactly once" is mostly marketing; what real systems do is <em>at-least-once delivery plus idempotent consumers</em>.</li>
<li><strong>Ordering is limited.</strong> Global ordering across a topic is expensive; most brokers only guarantee order within a partition or key. Design so that out-of-order arrivals are survivable.</li>
<li><strong>Failed messages need a home.</strong> A message that keeps failing should land in a <strong>dead-letter queue</strong> rather than blocking the line forever.</li>
</ul>
<p>The practical consequence is one rule worth tattooing on the team: <strong>every consumer must be safe to run twice.</strong> That property is called <em>idempotency</em>, and it is the difference between a robust system and one that emails the customer three receipts.</p>

<pre><code><span class="cmt">// Not idempotent: a duplicate delivery double-charges and double-emails</span>
<span class="kw">async function</span> onOrderPlaced(evt) {
  <span class="kw">await</span> payment.charge(evt.amount)        <span class="cmt">// runs again on redelivery</span>
  <span class="kw">await</span> email.sendReceipt(evt.customerId)
}

<span class="cmt">// Idempotent: the event id is the guard. Safe to run twice.</span>
<span class="kw">async function</span> onOrderPlaced(evt) {
  <span class="kw">if</span> (<span class="kw">await</span> seen.has(evt.id)) <span class="kw">return</span>   <span class="cmt">// already handled — do nothing</span>
  <span class="kw">await</span> payment.charge(evt.amount)
  <span class="kw">await</span> email.sendReceipt(evt.customerId)
  <span class="kw">await</span> seen.add(evt.id)
}</code></pre>

<h2 id="choreography-vs-orchestration">Who is in charge: choreography vs. orchestration</h2>
<p>Once work spans several services, something has to coordinate it. There are two philosophies, and the choice shapes how you will debug at 2&nbsp;a.m.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Choreography.</strong> No conductor. Each service listens for events and reacts, emitting its own events in turn. Order emits <code>OrderPlaced</code>; payment hears it and emits <code>PaymentTaken</code>; shipping hears <em>that</em>. Beautifully decoupled — and genuinely hard to follow, because the "flow" lives in no single place.</li>
<li><strong>Orchestration.</strong> A coordinator (an "order saga") explicitly tells each service what to do next and tracks progress. Easier to see and to reason about; the trade-off is that the coordinator becomes a thing you must own and keep simple.</li>
</ul>
<div class="blog-callout blog-callout--note"><span class="blog-callout__label">A useful rule of thumb</span><p>Use <strong>choreography</strong> for loose "fan-out" reactions where no one needs to know the whole story (analytics, notifications, search indexing). Use <strong>orchestration</strong> for a real business transaction with steps that must succeed or be undone together — which is exactly the <em>saga</em> problem we unpack in the next part on data.</p></div>

<h2 id="when-it-helps">Where event-driven architecture earns its keep</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Fan-out.</strong> One thing happens and many unrelated reactions follow. Adding the seventh reaction should not mean editing the thing that happened.</li>
<li><strong>Spiky or slow work.</strong> Video encoding, PDF generation, sending a million emails — push it onto a queue and let workers chew through it without making the user wait.</li>
<li><strong>Buffering load.</strong> A queue absorbs a traffic spike that would otherwise knock over a synchronous downstream service.</li>
<li><strong>Decoupling teams.</strong> The team that owns "orders" can publish facts without learning about every team that consumes them.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="when-not">When NOT to reach for events</h2>
<p>Events are not free, and reaching for them too early is one of the most common ways to manufacture accidental complexity.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>You need an immediate answer.</strong> "Is this coupon valid?" is a question, not an announcement. Just call the service.</li>
<li><strong>It is a two-service app.</strong> A broker, a schema registry, and a dead-letter queue are a lot of machinery to avoid a direct call between your only two services.</li>
<li><strong>The team has never operated a broker.</strong> Async failures are invisible by default. Without tracing and good dashboards, a stuck queue is a silent outage you discover from angry customers.</li>
<li><strong>Strong consistency is mandatory.</strong> If the business cannot tolerate "true in a second or two," eventual consistency will hurt — see the next part.</li>
</ul>
<div class="blog-callout blog-callout--warn"><span class="blog-callout__label">The "everything is an event" trap</span><p>The failure mode is turning every function call into an event and ending up with a system whose logic is scattered across twenty subscribers and impossible to trace. Asynchrony is a tool for <em>decoupling things that are genuinely independent</em> — not a default for everything. If you cannot explain a flow without a whiteboard and four colours, you have over-applied it.</p></div>

<h2 id="by-company-size">The honest view by company size</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Solo / early startup.</strong> You almost certainly do not need a broker yet. A background job table in your existing database (poll a <code>jobs</code> table, mark rows done) covers 90% of "do this later" needs with none of the operational weight.</li>
<li><strong>Growing scale-up.</strong> Introduce a managed broker (SQS, Pub/Sub, a hosted Kafka) for the two or three genuine fan-out points: order events, file processing, notifications. Keep synchronous calls for everything that needs an answer. Invest in tracing <em>before</em> you invest in more topics.</li>
<li><strong>Enterprise.</strong> Events become the backbone between teams, with schema governance, dead-letter handling, replay tooling, and explicit ownership of each topic. The hard part is no longer technology; it is <em>contracts</em> — agreeing on what an event means and never breaking it casually.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="getting-started">How to start on Monday</h2>
<ol>
<li>Find one place where a caller is waiting on work the user does not need to wait for — sending email, generating a report, syncing to a third party. That is your first event.</li>
<li>Publish a past-tense fact (<code>OrderPlaced</code>), not a command. Let the email service subscribe instead of being called.</li>
<li>Make the consumer idempotent from day one, keyed on the event id. Assume it will be delivered twice.</li>
<li>Add a dead-letter queue and an alert on it <em>before</em> you go live. A growing dead-letter queue is your earliest warning sign.</li>
<li>Add tracing so a single request can be followed across the hop. Async without observability is debugging blindfolded.</li>
</ol>

  <figcaption>Fan-out only stays healthy when someone still owns the story of the flow: what gets published, who reacts, and which steps are allowed to wait.</figcaption>
</figure>

<div class="blog-takeaways">
<h3 id="takeaways">Key takeaways</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Sync is a phone call; async is a letter.</strong> Use synchronous calls when you need the answer to continue, and messaging when you are announcing that something happened.</li>
  <li><strong>Commands ask; events announce.</strong> The command/event distinction decides who is in charge — get it right before anything else.</li>
  <li><strong>Brokers promise at-least-once, not exactly-once.</strong> Therefore every consumer must be idempotent and safe to run twice. This is non-negotiable.</li>
  <li><strong>Choreography decouples; orchestration clarifies.</strong> Fan-out reactions love choreography; real multi-step transactions want an orchestrator — the saga of the next part.</li>
  <li><strong>Events are for genuine independence, not for everything.</strong> Reaching for a broker in a two-service app, or turning every call into an event, manufactures the very complexity you were trying to avoid.</li>
</ul>
</div>
<p>Splitting the work was the easy half. The moment services stop sharing a database, a harder question arrives: <em>who owns the data, and what does "true" even mean when the truth is spread across five services?</em> That is the next part of this series.</p>
