10:T350b,<p>The startup promise is intoxicating: no bureaucracy, real ownership, ship something today, watch it matter tomorrow. A lot of that is true. What the recruiting page leaves out is the other side of the same coin — the ambiguity, the whiplash, the weeks you spend doing things you're not good at, and the quiet knowledge that the whole thing might not exist in a year.</p>
<p>Both sides are real, and they come from one root cause: a startup is a company still searching for a repeatable, profitable business. It hasn't earned the certainty that lets a big company specialise and systematise. Everything distinctive about working there — the freedom and the chaos — flows from that single fact. This deep-dive is an honest guide to the trade, covering both small startups (a handful of people, pre-product-market-fit) and mid-size ones (scaling, ~30–150 people), where the rules quietly start to change.</p>
<p>It's the startup chapter of the series that opened with the <a href="/blog/ways-of-working/how-teams-work-big-corp-startup-outsourcing">big corp vs startup vs outsourcing overview</a>.</p>

<h2>The one fact that explains everything</h2>
<p>A big company knows what it sells and to whom; its job is to do that reliably at scale. A startup doesn't know yet — it's running experiments to <em>find out</em>. That changes the goal of your work entirely:</p>
<div class="blog-callout blog-callout--note"><span class="blog-callout__label">Speed of learning &gt; quality of output</span><p>In a startup, the most valuable thing isn't a polished feature — it's <strong>information about what works</strong>. A scrappy version shipped this week that teaches you customers don't want it beats a beautiful version shipped next quarter that teaches you the same thing three months late. Once you internalise that the product is learning, not code, most startup decisions make sense.</p></div>
<p>This is why startups tolerate — even prize — things that would horrify a big corp: skipping process, cutting scope, shipping the rough version, throwing away last month's code. They're not being sloppy (when they're healthy); they're optimising for the only currency that matters before product-market fit: learning per week.</p>

<h2>Wide and shallow: wearing many hats</h2>
<p>In a big corp you own a deep, narrow slice and lean on specialists. A startup inverts this. The same week you might:</p>
<ul>
<li>Build a backend endpoint in the morning and fix the CSS in the afternoon.</li>
<li>Set up the deploy pipeline because nobody else has.</li>
<li>Get on a call with a frustrated customer and turn the bug they hit into your next ticket.</li>
<li>Weigh in on pricing, hiring, or which feature to cut.</li>
</ul>
<p>You own <strong>outcomes, not tickets</strong>. The reward is range and visible impact: you can see your work in customers' hands within days, and your fingerprints are on the whole product. The tax is that you're constantly operating slightly outside your expertise, on top of a codebase only one or two people fully understand. Comfort with "I'll figure it out" is the core startup temperament. If you need to be the expert before you act, this environment will be painful.</p>

<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-figure">
  <img src="/assets/blog/working-in-a-startup/ambiguity-into-next-bet.png" width="1440" height="960" loading="lazy" decoding="async" alt="Phong turns a fuzzy startup problem into a next step while two teammates challenge the idea with notes, sketches, and rough charts on the table." />
  <figcaption>In a healthy startup, ambiguity turns into motion when someone can frame the next small bet clearly enough for the team to challenge and ship it.</figcaption>
</figure>

<h2>Ambiguity is the job, not a bug</h2>
<p>New startup engineers often wait to be told what to do, the way they were in school or a big company, and then feel adrift when nobody tells them. The reframe that unlocks everything: <strong>ambiguity isn't a failure of management — it's the work being handed to you.</strong> Nobody knows the answer yet; your job is to help find it.</p>
<p>Concretely, that means:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Define your own scope.</strong> Given a fuzzy goal ("reduce churn"), break it into something shippable this week and propose it, rather than waiting for a spec.</li>
<li><strong>Decide and move.</strong> Most decisions are reversible. A made decision you can correct beats a perfect decision you never reach.</li>
<li><strong>Ask "what's the cheapest way to learn this?"</strong> before "what's the right way to build this?"</li>
</ul>
<div class="blog-callout blog-callout--tip"><span class="blog-callout__label">The startup superpower</span><p>The most valuable startup engineers don't ask "what should I work on?" They show up with "here's the most important thing I think we're missing, here's the cheap version I'd ship to test it, here's why." Taking ambiguous goals and turning them into concrete shipped bets is the single skill that compounds fastest.</p></div>

<h2>Speed vs debt: the tension you live in</h2>
<p>The startup's need for speed and its need to survive its own code pull against each other constantly. The resolution isn't "never take debt" or "always move fast" — it's <strong>taking debt consciously</strong>.</p>
<div class="blog-table-wrap">
<table>
<thead><tr><th width="32%">Kind of debt</th><th>Verdict</th><th>Why</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>Deliberate &amp; visible</strong> ("hardcode it now, we'll generalise if it works")</td><td>Healthy</td><td>You're buying speed knowingly; you can pay it back if the bet pays off</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Accidental &amp; invisible</strong> (mess nobody decided on or tracks)</td><td>Dangerous</td><td>It compounds silently until the team can't ship; this is how startups die <em>after</em> surviving</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Debt on the core</strong> (the part that survives every pivot)</td><td>Pay down early</td><td>The 20% that won't be thrown away deserves real care</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Debt on experiments</strong> (likely to be deleted)</td><td>Embrace it</td><td>Polishing code you'll throw away next month is the real waste</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
</div>
<p>The judgment call — which code is core and which is disposable — is what separates a senior startup engineer from a junior one. Get it right and you move fast without drowning. Get it wrong in either direction (gold-plating throwaways, or rotting the core) and the team pays for months.</p>

<h2>Communication when everyone's in the room</h2>
<p>Small teams run on high-bandwidth, low-ceremony communication: you turn around and ask, decisions happen in a hallway, the "standup" is a Slack thread. It's wonderfully fast. It also has a failure mode that's invisible until it bites: <strong>knowledge that lives only in people's heads and DMs.</strong></p>
<p>When the team is five, that's fine — everyone knows everything. When it doubles to ten, then twenty, the unwritten knowledge doesn't scale, and suddenly nobody knows why a decision was made or how a system works, and the original people are too busy to re-explain. The best small teams start writing the few things that matter — key decisions, how to run the system, why the architecture is the way it is — <em>before</em> the pain arrives, not after. You don't need big-corp documentation; you need just enough that the team's memory survives its own growth.</p>

<h2>The small-to-mid transition: when the rules change</h2>
<p>Something disorienting happens as a startup scales past ~30 people: the things that made it great start to break, and process — the very thing people joined to escape — begins to reappear. This isn't betrayal; it's necessity. Coordination that was free at five people costs real effort at fifty.</p>
<div class="blog-table-wrap">
<table>
<thead><tr><th width="34%">What changes</th><th>Early startup</th><th>Scaling (mid-size)</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>Decisions</strong></td><td>Anyone, instantly</td><td>Need light alignment; more people affected</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Process</strong></td><td>Almost none</td><td>Lightweight Scrum, on-call, basic reviews appear</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Roles</strong></td><td>Everyone does everything</td><td>Specialisation begins; first dedicated infra/QA/PM hires</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Knowledge</strong></td><td>In a few heads</td><td>Must be written down or it's lost</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
</div>
<p>Some early engineers hate this phase and leave for the next tiny startup; others grow into the leaders who build the structure. Neither is wrong — but knowing the transition is coming lets you choose deliberately instead of feeling like the company you joined disappeared. If you find you crave that structure rather than resent it, that's a signal a <a href="/blog/ways-of-working/working-in-a-big-corp">larger company</a> might suit you well.</p>

<h2>The failure modes to watch for</h2>
<div class="blog-table-wrap">
<table>
<thead><tr><th width="26%">Failure mode</th><th>What it looks like</th><th>The antidote</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>Burnout</strong></td><td>"Crunch" as a permanent state; heroics expected nightly</td><td>Sustainable pace is a feature; protect rest, and treat constant crunch as a red flag, not a badge</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Hero culture</strong></td><td>One person holds it all together; bus factor of one</td><td>Spread knowledge; write down the critical paths; resist being the indispensable hero</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Priority whiplash</strong></td><td>Today's #1 is forgotten by Friday</td><td>Push for a short, written, agreed focus; make the cost of switching visible</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Chaos mistaken for speed</strong></td><td>Lots of motion, little shipped or learned</td><td>Tie work to a hypothesis and a metric; busy is not the same as effective</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Equity dazzle</strong></td><td>Underpaying yourself on a lottery ticket</td><td>Value equity soberly; most options are worth little — join for the role and learning, not just the upside</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
</div>

<h2>How to thrive (and grow) in a startup</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Optimise for learning velocity</strong> — yours and the company's. Ship the cheap test; treat your own rapid skill growth as part of the comp.</li>
<li><strong>Own outcomes, not tasks.</strong> Bring problems and proposed bets, not just finished tickets. Initiative is the currency.</li>
<li><strong>Be senior about debt.</strong> Take it consciously on throwaways; protect the core. This judgment is what gets you trusted with bigger decisions.</li>
<li><strong>Write down just enough.</strong> Be the person whose work survives the team doubling.</li>
<li><strong>Grow into the structure.</strong> When the scaling phase arrives, the engineers who help build the lightweight process — instead of resenting it — become the de facto leaders.</li>
</ul>
<p>Career growth in a startup is fuzzy and fast: titles can inflate, scope can land on you overnight, and there's rarely a calibrated ladder. The upside is that impact is visible and proximity to the business can launch you into leadership years earlier than a big company would. The trade is real, and so is the variety on offer in <a href="/blog/ways-of-working/working-in-outsourcing-software-services">outsourcing</a> if you want breadth without betting your week on one product's survival.</p>

<div class="blog-takeaways">
<h3 id="takeaways">Key takeaways</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>A startup is still searching for its business</strong> — so the goal of your work is learning, not polish. Speed of learning beats quality of output before product-market fit.</li>
<li><strong>Ownership is wide and shallow.</strong> You wear many hats and own outcomes, constantly working just outside your expertise. "I'll figure it out" is the core temperament.</li>
<li><strong>Ambiguity is the work, not a management failure.</strong> Define your own scope, decide and move, and ask for the cheapest way to learn.</li>
<li><strong>Take debt consciously.</strong> Embrace it on disposable experiments; pay it down on the core that survives every pivot.</li>
<li><strong>Write down just enough</strong> before growth makes head-only knowledge a liability.</li>
