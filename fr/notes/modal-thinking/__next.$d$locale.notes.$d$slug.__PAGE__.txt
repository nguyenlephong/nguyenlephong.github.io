13:T90a,<p>The idea is simple but freeing: keep deciding and doing in two separate modes, and never let them run at the same time.</p>
<h2 id="decision-mode">Decision mode</h2>
<ul>
<li>Be empirical: lean on the decisions (avoidance included) that made you regret before. Your own history is the cheapest data you have.</li>
<li>An arbitrary decision is still a hundredfold better than an impulsive one. A choice you can stand behind beats a reaction you were dragged into.</li>
</ul>
<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-panorama">
  <img src="https://nguyenlephong.github.io/dom-pub/icdn/notes/modal-thinking/decision-mode-evidence.webp" width="1600" height="900" loading="lazy" decoding="async" alt="A focused professional reviews blank note cards and a notebook before choosing the next action." />
  <figcaption>Decision mode gets calmer when the evidence from your own history is on the table before the next move is chosen.</figcaption>
</figure>
<h2 id="execution-mode">Execution mode</h2>
<ul>
<li>You have no other option. Once you have decided, the deciding is over — there is nothing left to weigh.</li>
<li>You don&rsquo;t stop in the middle.</li>
<li>Whether it was a great or a bad move is for decision mode to judge — later, in reflection, with a calm head. Not mid-stride.</li>
<li>Even when it turns out to be a bad decision, the follow-through strengthens your adherence, your self-worth, and your trust in the heuristic — a long-term yield. You are training the muscle of following through, and that compounds.</li>
</ul>
<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-panorama">
  <img src="https://nguyenlephong.github.io/dom-pub/icdn/notes/modal-thinking/execution-mode-follow-through.webp" width="1600" height="900" loading="lazy" decoding="async" alt="A professional works under a desk lamp with a closed notebook beside the laptop, following through after the decision is made." />
  <figcaption>Execution mode protects the work from being reopened every few minutes; the review can wait until the stride is finished.</figcaption>
</figure>
<p>This way you save an enormous amount of mental energy from bounced thinking — the exhausting loop of reconsidering a choice while you are supposed to be acting on it.</p>
