<p><em>At a big corp, nobody hands you a leadership seat because you “have potential.” Potential is what people call you while you’re young. At the C-level, the market only looks at evidence.</em></p>
</blockquote>

<p>Don’t be surprised when you hear it said plainly: in a large corporation, the climb to the top isn’t won by promise. Early in a career, “high potential” is a compliment that opens doors. Near the executive floor, it stops meaning much. The questions get blunt and they get specific:</p>
<ul>
<li>Have you ever owned a <strong>P&amp;L</strong>?</li>
<li>Have you been personally accountable for <strong>revenue</strong>?</li>
<li>Do you understand finance, operations, people, technology <em>and</em> the market — together?</li>
<li>Can you decide well when the <strong>data is imperfect</strong>?</li>
<li>Are you someone others will trust to run a large, complex system?</li>
</ul>
<p>So the real question — “what do C-level leaders at a big corp actually learn?” — was never only about degrees. It’s a question about how a person gets <strong>trained to move from being a specialist to being a decision-maker</strong>. This is a tour of the five well-worn roads that get people there, the honest truth about the MBA, and the one mindset shift underneath all of it.</p>

<h2 id="potential-vs-evidence">Potential vs. evidence: the shift no one warns you about</h2>
<p>Here is the quiet reframe that explains everything below. A career changes what it rewards as you climb, and most people don’t notice until they’re stuck:</p>
<ul>
<li>As a <strong>specialist</strong>, you’re rewarded for <em>doing the work well</em>.</li>
<li>As a <strong>manager</strong>, you’re rewarded for <em>solving problems</em>.</li>
<li>As a <strong>C-level leader</strong>, you’re rewarded for being someone others will <em>bet on</em> — with their capital, their careers, their company.</li>
</ul>
<p>That last jump is the hard one, because it can’t be earned with effort alone. Trust at that altitude is built from evidence: a track record of decisions that worked, ownership of outcomes that carried real money and real risk. The five roads below are simply five different ways people accumulate that evidence — and then converge on the same final test.</p>

<h2 id="route-fmcg">Road 1 — FMCG → Brand / Sales / Category → General Management</h2>
<p>Look at the big consumer-goods groups and you’ll find a striking number of senior leaders who came up through <strong>brand management, sales, trade marketing, category management, or commercial</strong>. It’s one of the most unforgiving leadership academies on earth, because FMCG won’t let you hide behind pretty words. Did the product sell? Did share grow? Is there any margin left? Is the distribution channel actually working? Did the campaign move the number?</p>
<p>If you start in FMCG marketing, the first lesson isn’t how to make a great campaign — it’s how to see a campaign as one part of a <em>business</em> problem. Instead of awareness, you learn to think in penetration, conversion, and repeat purchase. Instead of social content, you think market share, channel performance, and sales uplift. Instead of brand love, you think pricing, margin, and category growth. People who reach C-level here learn the business fundamentals deeply: <strong>P&amp;L, consumer insight, distribution, pricing, sales operations, supply chain, and portfolio strategy.</strong> An MBA or Master in Management can help later — but only if it teaches you to read a company as a money-making system, not a communications machine.</p>

<h2 id="route-tech">Road 2 — Tech → Product / Engineering / Data → Business Leadership</h2>
<p>In tech, many executives didn’t start in business at all. They came from engineering, product, data, or operations. But to climb from <strong>technical expert to C-level, being brilliant at the craft isn’t enough</strong> — you have to learn to connect technology to a market.</p>
<p>A great engineer can build the product. A great product manager can perfect the experience. But a tech executive has to answer a bigger question: does this <em>scale</em>, does it <em>make money</em>, does it <em>retain</em> users, and is it worth continuing to burn capital on? So the people who rise learn product strategy, platform and unit economics, data analytics, pricing, growth, AI governance, and regulatory risk. This is why so many technologists eventually do an MBA, Executive MBA, or a tech-management program — not because they lack intelligence, but because <strong>technical intelligence and business acumen are two different things.</strong> Tech is not short of brilliant people; it’s short of people who understand the product, the money, <em>and</em> the humans at once. If you want the executive floor, don’t just learn one more programming language — learn to read cohort retention, CAC, LTV, burn rate, runway, gross margin, pricing models, and go-to-market strategy. That’s the language of leadership.</p>

<h2 id="route-finance">Road 3 — Finance → Audit / IB / Risk / Corporate Finance → CFO / CEO</h2>
<p>Finance is the field least forgiving of vagueness. If FMCG asks “will it sell?” and tech asks “will it scale?”, finance asks <strong>where is the money going, where does the risk sit, and will this decision destroy capital?</strong> Many finance C-levels and big-corp CFOs come up through audit, corporate finance, investment banking, private equity, risk management, or FP&amp;A.</p>
<p>On this road, credentials carry visible weight. A <strong>Master in Finance, MBA, CFA, ACCA, or CPA</strong> isn’t profile decoration — it’s how the market checks whether you have the foundation to sit in rooms where every decision has a price tag. But to reach CFO you can’t merely be “good with numbers.” You have to turn numbers into decisions. A great CFO doesn’t just report the past — <strong>a great CFO warns about the future.</strong></p>

<h2 id="route-consulting">Road 4 — Consulting → Strategy → Corporate Leadership</h2>
<p>Another very common route runs from consulting into corporate strategy and then into leadership. Consulting is a powerful school of <strong>structured thinking</strong>: you get fluent at breaking down problems, analysing data, building strategy, presenting to executives, and performing under pressure. Someone from McKinsey, BCG, Bain, or a similar strategy firm often arrives in a corporate role already knowing how to ask the right questions: Where does the problem <em>really</em> sit? Did revenue fall on volume, price, or mix? Did profit drop on cost, margin, or channel? Is the new market worth entering? Does this M&amp;A create value? Should we grow through product, channel, or geography?</p>
<p>But consulting has a ceiling. Being great at analysis doesn’t guarantee you’re great at <em>operating</em>. To truly make C-level, ex-consultants have to prove they can do more than build a deck — they have to <strong>execute, manage people, own results, and live with their decisions</strong> long after the strategy presentation ends. An MBA shows up a lot on this road, especially for those entering international consulting or pivoting from a specialism into strategy. But the MBA is no magic spell; it just opens the door a little wider for people whose foundation is already strong enough to walk through it.</p>

<h2 id="route-legal">Road 5 — Legal / Risk / Compliance → Governance → Executive Leadership</h2>
<p>This is the least-discussed road, and a rising one. In large corporations — especially banks, insurers, pharma, energy, tech, and any heavily-regulated industry — leaders don’t only need to grow; they need to know <strong>where they’re not allowed to be wrong.</strong> People who rise from legal, compliance, public policy, risk, or governance have an edge in complex environments. They understand the rules of the game, they know the limits of growth, and they can see the moment a business decision could turn into a legal crisis.</p>
<p>The academic base here is usually law, economics, public policy, finance, or risk management, often topped up later with an MBA, executive education, or a corporate-governance program. If the other roads teach you how to grow, <strong>this one teaches you how to keep the company from blowing up</strong> — and at a big corp, survival is sometimes a leadership skill in its own right.</p>

<p>Five very different starting points, one shared destination. What changes is the question each road trains you to answer obsessively:</p>

<tr><td><strong>FMCG</strong></td><td>“Does it sell — and is there margin?”</td><td>P&amp;L, consumer insight, distribution, pricing, sales ops, supply chain, portfolio strategy.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Tech</strong></td><td>“Does it scale and make money?”</td><td>Product &amp; unit economics, retention/CAC/LTV, pricing, growth, go-to-market, AI &amp; regulatory risk.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Finance</strong></td><td>“Where’s the money and the risk?”</td><td>Turning numbers into decisions: FP&amp;A, capital, risk — plus CFA/ACCA/CPA-level rigour.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Consulting</strong></td><td>“What’s the real problem?”</td><td>Structured problem-solving and strategy — then proof you can execute, not just present.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Legal &amp; Risk</strong></td><td>“Where can we not afford to fail?”</td><td>The rules, the limits of growth, governance, and seeing crises before they happen.</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
</div>

<h2 id="do-you-need-mba">So do you actually need a Master’s or MBA?</h2>
<p>The honest answer is: it’s a signal, not a requirement — and it only counts when it sits on top of real capability. A few data points worth holding together:</p>
<ul>
<li>According to <strong>GMAC</strong>, among the top 30 companies in the Fortune 500, 13 CEOs hold an MBA. That doesn’t make an MBA mandatory — but it shows that at the very top, it still carries weight as a signal.</li>
<li><strong>Harvard Business Review</strong> and related research note that the value of the credential is shifting: employers increasingly weigh skills, adaptability, and real performance over the name of a school. The degree still has value — but mostly when it travels with genuine ability.</li>
<li><strong>McKinsey</strong>’s work on the foundational skills for the future of work (its “DELTAs”) points to capabilities like critical thinking, problem-solving, communication, self-leadership, digital fluency, and adaptability — exactly what a C-level needs, regardless of whether they came from FMCG, Tech, Finance, or Consulting.</li>
</ul>

<div class="blog-callout blog-callout--tip"><span class="blog-callout__label">The honest verdict on the MBA</span><p>A Master’s or MBA <strong>doesn’t replace capability</strong> — but it can <em>systematise</em> the capability you have, expand your network, reposition your career, and get you into hiring markets where you’d otherwise be invisible. Study because a program answers questions your current career can’t — not to add one more line to a CV.</p></div>

<h2 id="trusted-not-hired">C-level don’t study to get a job. They study to be trusted.</h2>
<p>Step back and the whole picture resolves into one sentence. At each altitude, the purpose of learning changes:</p>

<div class="blog-table-wrap">
<table>
<thead><tr><th width="34%">At this level…</th><th>…you learn to:</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>Individual contributor</strong></td><td>get the work done.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Manager</strong></td><td>solve problems.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>C-level</strong></td><td>make others willing to bet on you.</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
</div>

<p>That is an enormous difference. You don’t need a Master’s just to add a line to your résumé. You need one if — and only if — it helps you answer the questions your current career hasn’t been able to: <strong>Do you understand how the business actually makes money? Can you read a P&amp;L? Do you decide from data? Do you understand the market, the customer, the product, the people, and the risk? Do you have enough systems thinking to avoid optimising one department while breaking the whole company?</strong></p>
<p>C-level leaders at a big corp don’t arrive on the strength of a single certificate. They arrive on <strong>layers of evidence</strong>: education, experience, results, network, leadership, and a demonstrated willingness to be accountable. So the question to retire is “do C-levels have a Master’s?” The better question is the one that actually shapes a career:</p>

<blockquote>
<p><em>If one day you want to sit in that chair — what are you learning right now that would make other people believe you’ve earned it?</em></p>
</blockquote>

<div class="blog-takeaways">
<h3 id="takeaways">Key takeaways</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>At the top, evidence beats potential.</strong> “High potential” opens early doors; the executive floor asks what you’ve actually owned — P&amp;L, revenue, hard decisions.</li>
<li><strong>The real curriculum is the shift from specialist to decision-maker</strong> — learning to read the whole business, not just your function.</li>
<li><strong>Five roads, one test:</strong> FMCG, Tech, Finance, Consulting, and Legal/Risk all converge on turning deep expertise into business decisions others will bet on.</li>
<li><strong>Each road drills one question:</strong> does it sell? does it scale? where’s the risk? what’s the real problem? where can we not fail?</li>
<li><strong>An MBA is a signal, not a substitute.</strong> It systematises capability, widens your network, and opens hiring markets — but only on top of genuine ability.</li>
<li><strong>Learning changes purpose by altitude:</strong> do the work → solve problems → be trusted. The top rung is earned in layers of evidence, not one certificate.</li>
<li><strong>The question that shapes a career:</strong> what are you learning now that would make others believe you’ve earned the chair?</li>
</ul>
</div>
