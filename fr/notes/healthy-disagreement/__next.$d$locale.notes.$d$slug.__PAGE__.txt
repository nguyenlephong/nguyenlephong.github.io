16:T8e5,<p>When a room agrees with you completely, it is worth pausing before you take it as approval. One hundred percent agreement can mean the people receiving the idea don&rsquo;t think for themselves, aren&rsquo;t paying attention, or — worst of all — don&rsquo;t dare to speak up. Unbroken consensus often signals absence — of friction, of independent minds chewing on the same problem — rather than the presence of a genuinely good idea.</p>
<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-panorama">
  <img src="https://nguyenlephong.github.io/dom-pub/icdn/notes/healthy-disagreement/question-in-design-review.webp" width="1600" height="900" loading="lazy" decoding="async" alt="A Vietnamese design review team listens while one teammate raises a careful question beside a tablet prototype." />
  <figcaption>A little respectful friction can show that the room is awake, not that the idea is in danger.</figcaption>
</figure>
<p>The opposite extreme is no better. When almost nobody can meet you, the problem may not be your idea at all: there may be too many unknowns, too little shared background — or a debating culture where disagreeing has quietly become a way to score points rather than to find the truth. People end up arguing past each other from different maps.</p>
<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-figure">
  <img src="https://nguyenlephong.github.io/dom-pub/icdn/notes/healthy-disagreement/shared-map-review.webp" width="1440" height="960" loading="lazy" decoding="async" alt="Three Vietnamese teammates compare blank sketches and abstract cards to build shared context before deciding." />
  <figcaption>Disagreement becomes healthier when people stop defending separate maps and make the shared picture visible.</figcaption>
</figure>
<p>So neither pole is the goal. Picture a design review where every reviewer nods and signs off in minutes: it feels efficient, but more often it means the work was never truly examined. There is no &ldquo;right&rdquo; ratio of agreement — it shifts with the team, the topic, and the stakes. The point is simply that <strong>complete agreement is not necessarily a good sign</strong>.
