13:T893,<p>Why should I make highlights while reading? What should be highlighted? And how will they be used? Without an answer to these, highlighting quietly turns into a habit that feels productive while producing nothing.</p>
<h2 id="types">The types of highlight</h2>
<p><strong>Resonance</strong> — the classic meaning. These are the lines that connected me with the book, my own personal feeling toward them. A line lights up not because it is objectively important, but because it touched something already in me.</p>
<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-panorama">
  <img src="https://nguyenlephong.github.io/dom-pub/icdn/notes/purpose-of-highlights/resonance-reading-window.webp" width="1600" height="900" loading="lazy" decoding="async" alt="A Vietnamese reader pauses beside a window over a blurred book page with one soft highlight band and blank note cards nearby." />
  <figcaption>A resonant line earns its mark because it touches something already present in the reader.</figcaption>
</figure>
<p><strong>Asterisks</strong> — marks on ideas and concepts that need further digging to understand. These are not keepsakes; they are open loops, flags planted on the spots where I owe more thought.</p>
<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-figure">
  <img src="https://nguyenlephong.github.io/dom-pub/icdn/notes/purpose-of-highlights/asterisk-follow-up-cards.webp" width="1440" height="960" loading="lazy" decoding="async" alt="A Vietnamese reader arranges blank follow-up cards beside a blurred highlighted page with small abstract star marks." />
  <figcaption>An asterisk is less a keepsake than an open loop: a place where the reader still owes the idea more thought.</figcaption>
</figure>
<h2 id="when">When to highlight</h2>
<p>Highlights should serve a purpose of <strong>future action</strong>, not collection out of the <strong>fear of missing out</strong>. If a highlight has no future in it — nothing you will revisit, reuse, or act on — then you are only hoarding sentences.</p>
