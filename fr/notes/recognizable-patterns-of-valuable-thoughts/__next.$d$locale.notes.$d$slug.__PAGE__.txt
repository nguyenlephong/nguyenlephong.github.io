15:Ta41,<p>It is perhaps easier to spot low-value thoughts than to define the valuable ones — so let&rsquo;s start from the easier end. These are the mental motions that feel like thinking but rarely lead anywhere:</p>
<ul>
<li>Rewinding the past just for the sake of it.</li>
<li>Imagining the future just for the sake of it.</li>
<li>Self-imaging — glorifying yourself, through either the past or the future.</li>
<li>Repeating a problem that has no possible action attached.</li>
<li>The <em>Einstellung</em> rut — running the same thought again and again with no new insight, often around a problem that is simply intractable for now.</li>
<li>Problems that are not in your control.</li>
</ul>

<p>The common thread: the mind keeps spinning while the wheels never touch the road. The way off that train, as far as I can tell, runs through two related moves — settling into inner stillness, and stepping outside yourself to watch the thought as an observer rather than ride along inside it. Either one breaks the loop&rsquo;s grip long enough to ask: is this going anywhere?</p>
<p>And the probably-valuable thoughts? They share one property: they can lead somewhere — to an action, or to a new understanding. Consolidating and reciting what you learned recently is a good example of the latter. The real problem might simply be discipline: letting the low-value thoughts slide, and trusting yourself to know what is worth thinking about.</p>
