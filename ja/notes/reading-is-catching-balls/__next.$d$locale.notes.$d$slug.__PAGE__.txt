15:T9b1,<p>An analogy on reading that has stayed with me, from <em>How to Read a Book</em> by Adler &amp; Van Doren.</p>
<p>The relation between <strong>writer</strong> and <strong>reader</strong> is remarkably similar to the relation between <strong>pitcher</strong> and <strong>catcher</strong> in baseball. Both <em>pitching-writing</em> and <em>catching-reading</em> are <strong>active, demanding efforts</strong>. There is no such thing as passive catching, and no such thing as passive reading: the ball does not arrive in the mitt by itself, and meaning does not arrive in the mind by itself either.</p>
<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-panorama">
  <img src="https://nguyenlephong.github.io/dom-pub/icdn/notes/reading-is-catching-balls/active-reading-desk.webp" width="1600" height="900" loading="lazy" decoding="async" alt="A Vietnamese reader writes beside a blank open book, a notebook, a plain baseball glove, and a ball on a warm desk." />
  <figcaption>Reading becomes active when the reader reaches toward meaning instead of waiting for it to land by itself.</figcaption>
</figure>
<p>The success of the whole activity is how well the two cooperate. It is no win for the pitcher-writer to throw a <strong>ball so difficult</strong> that the catcher-reader simply cannot catch it — a brilliant point that lands nowhere has been wasted. Nor is it a win to throw a <strong>ball so easy</strong> it never gets past the batter — and in reading, the batter is boredom: too gentle, and the reader&rsquo;s attention swats it away.</p>
<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-figure">
  <img src="https://nguyenlephong.github.io/dom-pub/icdn/notes/reading-is-catching-balls/calibrated-catch-practice.webp" width="1440" height="960" loading="lazy" decoding="async" alt="Two Vietnamese friends practice a careful pitch and catch on a quiet field, with a blank notebook and open book resting nearby." />
  <figcaption>The best exchange sits between too easy and impossible, where the throw still asks the catcher to grow.</figcaption>
</figure>
<p>Pitcher-writer and catcher-reader are two people at different levels of expertise, and both keep practicing to level up. But the activity cannot be optimal when the <strong>disparity</strong> is too wide — a major-league throw is useless to a beginner&rsquo;s glove. Even so, a good pitcher-writer will usually throw the most <strong>challenging balls</strong> they responsibly can, trusting the reader to rise and meet them.</p>9:["$","$L14",null,{"html":"$15"}]
