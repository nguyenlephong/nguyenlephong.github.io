17:T640,<p>There is a particular kind of tiredness that has nothing to do with the work itself. It is the fatigue of the endless search — the open browser tabs comparing one app against another, the evenings lost to configuring a setup you will abandon within a month, the quiet conviction that the <em>right</em> tool is out there and you simply have not found it yet. Call it <strong>perfect-tools syndrome</strong>: the constant seeking, searching, and switching that wears the costume of progress while quietly standing in for it.</p>
<p>The pattern is subtle because it <em>feels</em> productive. Choosing tools is concrete and satisfying in a way the actual problem often is not. But a tool is only ever a means, and the moment the means starts dictating the ends, you have let the toolbox define the carpenter. The question &ldquo;which app should I use?&rdquo; quietly crowds out the one that matters: &ldquo;what am I actually trying to solve — and is it even worth solving?&rdquo;</p>
<p>So flip the order. Get clear on the worthy problem first — the one that has earned your attention — and let the tool fall out of it, instead of the other way around.</p>
<p><strong>The shift in one line</strong>: stop trying to <em>choose the best of</em>, and start trying to <em>make the most out of</em>. The sharpest people are rarely the ones with the most refined stack; they are the ones who can do remarkable work with whatever is already in their hands.</p>
