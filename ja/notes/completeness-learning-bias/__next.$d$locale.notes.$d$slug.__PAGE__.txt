15:T88e,<p>Courses and books are pathways that converge on the same truths. Missing a lecture or skipping a chapter does not mean a piece of knowledge is lost to you forever. It means you will arrive at the same truth through another path, eventually — the destination outlasts any single route to it.</p>
<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-panorama">
  <img src="https://nguyenlephong.github.io/dom-pub/icdn/notes/completeness-learning-bias/many-paths-same-truth.webp" width="1600" height="900" loading="lazy" decoding="async" alt="A Vietnamese adult pauses at a study desk with an open book, blank notes, a laptop, and cards arranged like several paths toward the same idea." />
  <figcaption>A skipped chapter feels less final when the desk starts to show more than one route toward the same understanding.</figcaption>
</figure>
<p>The bias is the nagging guilt that any gap in the curriculum is an irreversible loss. It makes you re-read chapters you half-remember, restart courses from the beginning, and treat the table of contents as a checklist rather than a map. But ideas are bigger than their representations. Two different textbooks, two different teachers, two different life experiences can all lead to the same understanding; the insight you missed in chapter four may simply be waiting for you in a conversation, a project, or a book you haven&rsquo;t opened yet.</p>
<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-figure">
  <img src="https://nguyenlephong.github.io/dom-pub/icdn/notes/completeness-learning-bias/conversation-project-new-route.webp" width="1440" height="960" loading="lazy" decoding="async" alt="Two Vietnamese professionals discuss a project beside an open book, blank notes, and a laptop with no readable screen content." />
  <figcaption>Sometimes the idea we missed in one representation returns through a conversation, a project, or another book.</figcaption>
</figure>
<p>So the useful move is not to chase completeness. It is to think hard about what truths hide behind the particular representation in front of you — and to trust that what matters will find another way in.</p>
