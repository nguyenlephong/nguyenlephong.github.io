18:T9ff,<p>Writing in this form is an honest way to spot the holes — the quiet disconnections in your understanding. You can nod along to an idea in your head and feel that you grasp it; the moment you try to set it down in full sentences, every gap announces itself. Serious note-takers call these <strong>permanent notes</strong>: notes meant to last, written to stand on their own.</p>
<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-panorama">
  <img src="https://nguyenlephong.github.io/dom-pub/icdn/notes/write-to-understand-and-transfer/gaps-on-the-page.webp" width="1600" height="900" loading="lazy" decoding="async" alt="Hands rewrite loose marks into clearer notes on an open notebook, showing how gaps become visible on the page." />
  <figcaption>The page makes understanding honest: vague confidence has to become visible enough to be questioned.</figcaption>
</figure>
<p>The process should feel <strong>effortful</strong>, and at the same time <strong>playful</strong>. Effortful, because you are deliberately filling gaps and forging meaningful connections to your existing ideas and mental models — real cognitive work that resists being rushed. Playful, because in the very act of doing it you stumble onto insights you did not know you had; the connections you forge end up teaching you something.</p>
<p>The objective is that your future self can read these notes back and still comprehend them — without finding them banal or empty. If a note fails that bar, it is a sign your threshold for concretizing the idea was set too low to ever really comprehend the subject. A note that reads as obvious later usually means the thought was never pinned down in the first place. That is why writing to understand should equal writing to transfer — whether the recipient is your future self or someone else entirely.</p>

  <figcaption>A durable note is generous to its next reader, even when that reader is only your future self.</figcaption>
