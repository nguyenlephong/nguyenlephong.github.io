18:T8aa,<p>Countless times you have heard the advice: &ldquo;you should write more.&rdquo; It is well-meant, and almost always true. But it skips the more useful question: write <em>what</em>? Writing is just an act, and an act can serve very different ends. Different kinds of writing have different <strong>purposes</strong>, demand a different and particular kind of <strong>attention</strong>, and are judged by different <strong>measures</strong> of result. Lumping them together is how people end up frustrated — pouring effort into one mode while quietly grading themselves by the standards of another.</p>
<p>So it helps to name the modes before chasing the volume. I split my own writing into two purposes: <a href="/notes/write-to-attend">writing to attend</a>, and <a href="/notes/write-to-understand-and-transfer">writing to understand and to transfer</a>. The first is writing as a way of being present with your own mind.</p>

<p>The second is writing as a way of building something durable that can travel to another person.</p>
