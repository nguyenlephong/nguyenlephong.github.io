18:Ta63,<p>Making software is like assembling Lego bricks.</p>
<p>At first, you learn the fundamental bricks — the small, primitive pieces everything else is eventually built from. Gradually, you learn the precomposed blocks made from those bricks: the abstractions built by others so you don&rsquo;t have to start from the studs each time.</p>
<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-panorama">
  <img src="https://nguyenlephong.github.io/dom-pub/icdn/notes/software-engineering-fatigue/abstraction-blocks-at-night.webp" width="1600" height="900" loading="lazy" decoding="async" alt="A Vietnamese software engineer studies layered toy-brick structures beside a laptop with abstract blocks on the screen." />
  <figcaption>The first bricks are easy to see; the fatigue begins when every useful block is built from another block you did not make.</figcaption>
</figure>
<p>More and more, as systems grow complicated, people compose blocks from blocks that were themselves built by others. Layer settles on top of layer, each one a convenience that hides the one beneath it.</p>
<p>Eventually you stray far from the fundamental bricks. The cost of finding out what bricks are actually down there becomes impractical — at least if your real goal is shipping sellable software on a deadline. And somewhere in that drift, your learning quietly changes character: from <em>understanding things</em> into <em>how to use things</em>. You don&rsquo;t learn the bricks anymore; you accept the blocks as handed to you. They are incidental; they feel artificial — and that artificiality is the source of the fatigue.</p>
<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-figure">
  <img src="https://nguyenlephong.github.io/dom-pub/icdn/notes/software-engineering-fatigue/lifting-the-layered-block.webp" width="1440" height="960" loading="lazy" decoding="async" alt="A Vietnamese engineer lifts the top layer of a toy-brick tower to reveal mixed pieces underneath beside a prism and pendulum." />
  <figcaption>Looking underneath has a cost: sometimes the layer you lift only reveals more layers, some useful and some merely inherited.</figcaption>
</figure>
<p>To put it in perspective: learning physics boils you down toward the truth of the universe — each thing you learn is a deeper layer of something real. Learning software engineering today mostly means learning arbitrary inventions of other people. They do not compound; one person&rsquo;s framework does not stack cleanly onto another&rsquo;s the way physical law does, so the effort rarely accumulates into something permanent.</p>
