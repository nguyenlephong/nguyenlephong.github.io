<p><em>Agent-enabled coding is becoming the new standard of software development. Every phase engineers once handled by hand — planning, coding, testing, deployment — agents can now execute, largely on their own.</em></p>
</blockquote>

<p>Software development just went through its biggest structural change since the graphical interface replaced the command line. For decades the work was a relay race: humans ran each phase, handed off to the next, and waited. Now a new pattern is taking shape, in which <strong>AI agents drive most of the execution</strong> — writing, refactoring, and testing in parallel — while engineers move up a level to set goals, steer, and verify.</p>
<p>The shorthand for the old model is <strong>SDLC</strong> (Software Development Life Cycle). The shorthand for what’s replacing it is <strong>ADLC</strong> — the <strong>Agentic Development Life Cycle</strong>. This is a calm, visual breakdown of exactly what changed, what it means for your day-to-day as an engineer, and how to adopt it without losing the judgment that still has to be human.</p>

<h2 id="biggest-shift">The biggest shift since the GUI</h2>
<p>The core change is simple to state and large to absorb: the unit of work moved from <em>a person doing a task</em> to <em>a person directing agents that do the tasks</em>. In the old world, every phase was locked, sequential, and human-driven — QA only happened after design, and changing requirements mid-cycle broke everything downstream. In the new world, agents write, refactor, and test in parallel, goals evolve as the work reveals itself, and feedback is live rather than retrospective.</p>
<p>That doesn’t mean engineers disappear. It means the centre of gravity of the job moves — from typing the implementation to defining the intent, orchestrating the agents, and judging the result. To see why, it helps to put the two lifecycles next to each other.</p>

<h2 id="sdlc-old-way">SDLC: the old way</h2>
<p>The classic lifecycle is a clockwise loop of eight phases, each one human-driven and, crucially, each one largely locked before the next begins.</p>

<p>Its strength is predictability; its weakness is rigidity. Because QA is a dedicated phase that arrives <em>after</em> design and implementation, problems are found late. Because scope is fixed up front, a change in requirements mid-cycle ripples backward and breaks plans. Learning is saved up for the retrospective at the very end — long after it could have helped.</p>

<h2 id="adlc-new-way">ADLC: the new way</h2>
<p>The Agentic Development Life Cycle keeps the shape of a loop but changes who does the work and when. Most phases are now executed by agents, often several in parallel — and one phase is deliberately kept human.</p>

<p>Read it clockwise. You <strong>define the goal</strong> (intent, outcomes, constraints), <strong>build a PRD</strong> the agents can execute against, <strong>write the skills</strong> (the tools, prompts, and capabilities they’ll use), and <strong>orchestrate the agents</strong>. From there the agents take over: <strong>autonomous coding</strong> (they write, refactor, and call tools) and <strong>autonomous testing</strong> (they run their own test suites and evals). Then comes the one step that stays firmly human — <strong>manual evaluation and observability</strong>, where you approve, correct, and steer — before <strong>deployment</strong> (auto CI/CD via agents) and continuous <strong>monitoring and feedback</strong> (live performance, drift detection) feed straight back into the goal. The loop never really stops; it just keeps tightening.</p>

<h2 id="six-shifts">The six shifts, side by side</h2>
<p>Strip it down and the transformation is six concrete changes. This is the heart of the difference:</p>

<h2 id="the-numbers">What the early numbers suggest</h2>
<p>This isn’t a forecast — it’s already underway in production teams. According to Anthropic’s reporting on agentic-coding trends, engineering teams at companies such as Wiz and CRED have reported roughly <strong>doubling their execution speed</strong> after adopting agent-driven workflows. In one widely-cited example, Claude Code reportedly ran <strong>autonomously for about seven hours</strong> to complete a complex implementation inside a codebase of roughly <strong>12.5 million lines</strong> at Rakuten.</p>

<div class="blog-callout blog-callout--note"><span class="blog-callout__label">Read the numbers honestly</span><p>Figures like these are real signals, but they’re best read as <em>direction</em>, not guarantees. They come from teams that already restructured how they work, on tasks suited to agents. Your mileage depends on the clarity of your goals, the quality of your tests, and how much you invest in the human evaluation step — not on the headline alone.</p></div>

<h2 id="for-engineers">What this actually means for engineers</h2>
<p>If your instinct is “does this replace me?”, the more useful framing is “this relocates me.” The repetitive execution — boilerplate, wiring, first-pass tests, routine refactors — moves to agents. What stays, and grows in value, is everything around it: defining crisp goals, designing the system, writing the skills agents use, and exercising the judgment to tell a good result from a confidently-wrong one.</p>
<p>The single biggest habit change is in <strong>review</strong>. In SDLC you reviewed code line by line. In ADLC, when agents can produce thousands of lines an hour, line-by-line review stops scaling. The job shifts to reviewing <em>outcomes and edge cases</em>: does it meet the goal, does it handle the nasty inputs, is it safe, is it maintainable? You stop being the person who writes every line and become the person accountable for whether the whole thing is right.</p>

<h2 id="best-practices">Five best practices to move faster</h2>
<p>The shift can feel daunting if you try to adopt all of it at once. Don’t. Here’s a low-risk path that compounds:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Start with one agent, on testing.</strong> Automating your testing phase first is the lowest-risk, highest-reward entry point — tests are checkable and contained, so mistakes are cheap and obvious.</li>
<li><strong>Learn to write PRDs and skills for agents.</strong> Goals need to be crisp, because agents execute exactly what you define. Vague intent in, vague work out; precise intent in, useful work out.</li>
<li><strong>Introduce parallel sub-agents.</strong> Take one large task and split it into three smaller, agent-run workstreams. Parallelism is where the speed actually comes from.</li>
<li><strong>Shift your review habit.</strong> Stop reviewing every line; start reviewing outcomes and edge cases. Your attention is the scarce resource — spend it where correctness is decided.</li>
<li><strong>Build feedback into the loop.</strong> Set up live monitoring so the agents flag drift and anomalies before you would have noticed them yourself.</li>
</ol>

<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-figure">

  <figcaption>The human checkpoint stays load-bearing: speed is useful only when someone still owns the final yes or no.</figcaption>
</figure>

<h2 id="caveats">The honest caveats</h2>
<div class="blog-callout blog-callout--warn"><span class="blog-callout__label">Why one step stays human</span><p>Notice that the ADLC loop keeps a deliberate human checkpoint — <strong>Manual Eval &amp; Observability</strong> — right before deployment. That’s not a transitional leftover; it’s load-bearing. Agents can be confidently wrong, and because they act in parallel and at speed, a flawed assumption can propagate fast. Keep approval on anything irreversible, keep observability live, and treat the agents like a brilliant, tireless team that still needs a senior signing off the release.</p></div>
<p>Two failure modes bracket the smart middle. One is dismissing all of this as hype and quietly falling behind teams who doubled their throughput. The other is over-trusting the agents and shipping fast, fluent, plausible mistakes. The whole craft of the ADLC era is living in between: adopt eagerly, define precisely, and verify relentlessly.</p>

<div class="blog-takeaways">
<h3 id="takeaways">Key takeaways</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>SDLC → ADLC is the biggest shift since the GUI.</strong> Work moves from a person doing tasks to a person directing agents that do them.</li>
<li><strong>SDLC is locked, sequential, human-driven;</strong> ADLC is parallel, dynamic, agent-driven, with live feedback instead of end-of-project retros.</li>
<li><strong>Six concrete shifts:</strong> driver, planning, development speed, testing, adaptability, and feedback loop all move from manual-and-sequential to agentic-and-continuous.</li>
<li><strong>The numbers are signals, not guarantees:</strong> reported speed doublings and long autonomous runs are real, but depend on goal clarity, test quality, and human evaluation.</li>
<li><strong>Engineers are relocated, not replaced:</strong> execution moves to agents; goal-setting, system design, skills, and judgment grow in value.</li>
<li><strong>Change your review habit:</strong> stop reviewing every line, start reviewing outcomes and edge cases — that’s where correctness is decided.</li>
<li><strong>Adopt in five low-risk steps,</strong> and keep one human checkpoint (eval &amp; observability) firmly in the loop. Adopt eagerly, verify relentlessly.</li>
</ul>
</div>
