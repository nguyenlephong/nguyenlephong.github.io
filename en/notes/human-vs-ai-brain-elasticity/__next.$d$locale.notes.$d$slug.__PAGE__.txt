18:T611,<p>For all the speed and breadth of modern AI, there is one place where models-as-software still fall short of the human brain: our &ldquo;wetware&rdquo; comes with a biological advantage of <strong>elasticity</strong>. The mind is not a fixed machine running fixed weights; it reshapes itself in the very act of being used.</p>
<p>AI models do not learn continuously. Once trained, their weights are frozen until someone deliberately retrains them. There is no equivalent of the mind&rsquo;s &ldquo;neurons that fire together, wire together&rdquo; — that quiet, ongoing rewiring that happens whether or not we notice it.</p>
<p>We carry a complicated biochemical machine that &ldquo;fine-tunes&rdquo; with every inference. Each time we think, recall, or struggle with something, information patterns are encoded and abstracted into efficient chunks automatically. Consider how a phone number you have dialed a hundred times becomes a single fluid gesture rather than ten separate digits — that compression happened on its own. An LLM, by contrast, starts from scratch no matter how many hours it has spent on the same problem; nothing from one conversation settles into the next.</p>
<p>And the difference runs deeper than memory. Agentic LLM behavior acquires contextual connections largely through semantic similarity of wording — matching things that <em>sound</em> alike.
