15:T52c,<p>When a room agrees with you completely, it is worth pausing before you take it as approval. One hundred percent agreement can mean the people receiving the idea don&rsquo;t think for themselves, aren&rsquo;t paying attention, or — worst of all — don&rsquo;t dare to speak up. Unbroken consensus often signals absence — of friction, of independent minds chewing on the same problem — rather than the presence of a genuinely good idea.</p>
<p>The opposite extreme is no better. When almost nobody can meet you, the problem may not be your idea at all: there may be too many unknowns, too little shared background — or a debating culture where disagreeing has quietly become a way to score points rather than to find the truth. People end up arguing past each other from different maps.</p>
<p>So neither pole is the goal. Picture a design review where every reviewer nods and signs off in minutes: it feels efficient, but more often it means the work was never truly examined. There is no &ldquo;right&rdquo; ratio of agreement — it shifts with the team, the topic, and the stakes. The point is simply that <strong>complete agreement is not necessarily a good sign</strong>.
