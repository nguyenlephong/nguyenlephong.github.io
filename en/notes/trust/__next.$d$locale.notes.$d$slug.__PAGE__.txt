8:Te9c,<p>There are a few rules of thumb I find universal, applicable to many corners of life. &ldquo;Trust by default&rdquo; is one of them.</p>
<h2 id="why-trust">Why does trust matter?</h2>
<p>It is a <em>heuristic</em> for freeing up mental capacity. Rather than re-litigating every assumption, trust gives you a stable basis to explore from and build upon.</p>
<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-panorama">
  <img src="https://nguyenlephong.github.io/dom-pub/icdn/notes/trust/stable-basis-for-trust.webp" width="1600" height="900" loading="lazy" decoding="async" alt="A professional works at a calm desk with a laptop, blank task cards, flashcards, and a closed notebook arranged as trusted systems." />
  <figcaption>Trust can work like a stable surface: enough structure to stop re-checking every small thing before moving forward.</figcaption>
</figure>
<p>And really — because there is no other way to live. Doubting everything by default will overwhelm you to a breaking point; the mind simply cannot carry that load. At the bottom of it there is a line you must draw, and you give the benefit of the doubt to the many things that fall beyond it.</p>
<h2 id="what-i-trust">What do I trust?</h2>
<ul>
<li>Personal task management — I trust my GTD system.</li>
<li>Evolving a codebase — I trust the unit test suites.</li>
<li>Working in an organized team — I trust the process.</li>
<li>Working with people — I trust them by default.</li>
<li>Learning — I trust my SRS to retain the facts.</li>
</ul>
<p>I also trust the yin-yang philosophy — that every rule has its exceptions (the grammar of languages is a prominent example):</p>
<ul>
<li>It&rsquo;s okay to skip your GTD cycles sometimes, if you genuinely enjoy whatever you&rsquo;re doing.</li>
<li>You shouldn&rsquo;t rely on unit tests as the sole safety net.</li>
<li>Don&rsquo;t follow a process arrogantly.</li>
<li>You probably shouldn&rsquo;t entrust strangers with important matters.</li>
<li>You should be ready to doubt what you&rsquo;ve long known.</li>
<li>I don&rsquo;t trust information that will eventually drive a decision — not without validating it first.</li>
</ul>
<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-figure">
  <img src="https://nguyenlephong.github.io/dom-pub/icdn/notes/trust/boundary-before-decision.webp" width="1440" height="960" loading="lazy" decoding="async" alt="A professional pauses over a blank checklist beside a laptop and papers, with a thin line drawn across the notebook page." />
  <figcaption>Boundaries keep trust from becoming blind; the pause is small, but it protects the decision that follows.</figcaption>
</figure>
<h2 id="square-one">Back to square one, aren&rsquo;t we?</h2>
<p>So there is a sliver of doubt tucked into the corner of every trust. Doesn&rsquo;t that make this whole piece basically pointless?</p>
<p>If there were a definite answer to whether to trust a thing, life would be easy. It would also be boring. Even an atom is not indivisible. There are many components inside any &ldquo;thing&rdquo; — many ideas in a book, many people in a team, many principles in a methodology — and trust rarely belongs to all of them at once.</p>
<p>We would love to hold one consistent, holistic view of everything that fits in a single word — but is <a href="https://www.goodreads.com/quotes/103022-you-can-know-the-name-of-a-bird-in-all">a &ldquo;bird&rdquo; really a bird?</a> A blindly unbending trust is, in Ralph Waldo Emerson&rsquo;s words, &ldquo;the hobgoblin of little minds.&rdquo; Life is fascinating precisely that way: an endless fractal to uncover, in which we can never absolutely trust that we are right.</p>
