13:T811,<p>Beyond our five senses there is an internal signal — a sixth one, if you like — that fires when the brain <em>feels</em> itself. A non-verbal, concrete flash to the conscious mind, lit in a color unique to the moment. You don&rsquo;t summon it. It arrives on its own terms, often before you have any words for it.</p>
<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-panorama">
  <img src="https://nguyenlephong.github.io/dom-pub/icdn/notes/sense/preverbal-signal-at-desk.webp" width="1600" height="900" loading="lazy" decoding="async" alt="A Vietnamese adult pauses at an evening desk with a blank notebook, face-down phone, tea, and a softly blurred laptop." />
  <figcaption>Some signals arrive before we have enough language to explain them.</figcaption>
</figure>
<p>These senses are the product of <strong>cumulative</strong> years of experience with long-term feedback. They are internalized thoughts, compressed below the surface of language — not sensors, but the residue of everything you have lived through, distilled into a feeling that knows before you can explain why.</p>
<p>There is a real difference between deliberately reasoning over a behavior and feeling the sense associated with it. Reasoning is expensive — like inference with full context overhead: slow, costly, deliberate. Sense is cheap — like a well-trained model responding straight from its weights: instant, almost effortless. But that training is only built one way: by acting first and reflecting after. Action precedes the sense, not the other way around.</p>
