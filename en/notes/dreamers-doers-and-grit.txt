<p><em>People can always find reasons to stop. The people who keep building return to the goal. The single difference between a person who only imagines and person who keeps building comes down to one stubborn word: grit.</em></p>
</blockquote>

<p>Somewhere right now, someone with half your talent is finishing the thing you keep meaning to start. It isn’t because they’re smarter, luckier, or better connected. It’s because when the work got boring, hard, and quietly humiliating, they kept going — and you, very reasonably, found a reason not to.</p>
<p>This isn’t a scolding. It’s an invitation. Because the gap between the life you imagine and the life you’re living is almost never made of ideas or ability. It’s made of one unglamorous quality the Vietnamese call <em>lì</em> — a kind of cheerful stubbornness, a refusal to stop. Let’s talk about why that one thing matters more than almost anything else, and how to build a little more of it, starting today.</p>

<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-panorama">
  <img src="https://nguyenlephong.github.io/dom-pub/icdn/notes/dreamers-doers-and-grit/first-step-at-desk.webp" width="1600" height="900" loading="lazy" decoding="async" alt="A Vietnamese professional sits at an evening desk with a laptop, blank notebook, coffee, and phone face down, preparing to start a difficult project." />
  <figcaption>A first step often looks quiet and ordinary: one page, one desk, and a decision to begin before confidence arrives.</figcaption>
</figure>

<h2 id="reasons-vs-goals">Reasons are easy to find; the goal needs to be protected</h2>
<p>Here is the pattern, and it is a convincing one: <strong>reasons are infinite, and they’re always available.</strong> Too busy. Too tired. Wrong timing. Not ready. The market’s crowded. I’ll start Monday. Each reason is individually true and collectively fatal. Your mind is a tireless factory for producing them, and it works overtime precisely when you’re closest to a breakthrough.</p>
<p>A goal is different. A goal is a single fixed point you keep walking toward while the reasons swirl. The doer isn’t someone who never feels like quitting — they feel it constantly. They’ve just decided that the goal outranks the reasons. When you have a real goal, “I don’t feel like it today” stops being a verdict and becomes just weather: noted, and walked through anyway.</p>

<div class="blog-callout blog-callout--note"><span class="blog-callout__label">Notice the factory</span><p>The next time you’re about to stop, listen to the reason your mind hands you. It will sound responsible, even wise. Ask it one question: <em>is this protecting me, or just protecting my comfort?</em> Most of the time, the “good reason” is comfort wearing a disguise.</p></div>

<h2 id="not-short-of-ideas">The world isn’t short of ideas — it’s short of follow-through</h2>
<p>There is no shortage of promising ideas. You’ve had a few yourself. So has everyone in your group chat. Ideas are the cheapest thing in the world; they cost nothing and arrive for free in the shower. What’s genuinely rare — and therefore valuable — is the person willing to walk through the long, unglamorous middle where the idea stops being exciting and starts being <em>work</em>.</p>
<p>That’s the real scarcity. Not vision. Not creativity. The ability to keep showing up through your own discouragement, on the days when nothing is working and no one is clapping. An average idea executed with grit beats a brilliant idea abandoned at the first hard week, every single time.</p>

<h2 id="think-do-persist">Think → do → persist: the three altitudes</h2>
<p>There’s an old line that maps the whole journey in three short moves, and it’s worth pinning to your wall:</p>

<div class="blog-table-wrap">
<table>
<thead><tr><th width="26%">The move</th><th width="22%">What it is</th><th>What it asks of you</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>To envision it</strong></td><td>Vision</td><td>The ability to see a future that doesn’t exist yet. Necessary — but the cheap part.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>To start doing it</strong></td><td>Mettle</td><td>The courage to act before you’re ready, to turn the idea into a clumsy first version.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>To keep going until it works</strong></td><td>Follow-through</td><td>The grit to stay with it through the difficult days — this is where almost everyone falls away.</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
</div>

<p>Most people are full of vision and short on the third line. They can see it; they may even start it; they just can’t outlast the valley. But notice the quiet promise hidden in that word <em>long-term outcome</em>: the outcome isn’t handed to the most gifted. It’s earned by whoever is still walking when the gifted have gone home.</p>

<h2 id="believe-then-do">First believe you can. Then just do. Then do it until you can.</h2>
<p>There’s a simple sequence underneath every hard thing anyone has ever pulled off. First, you have to <strong>believe it’s possible for you</strong> — not certain, just possible. Then you <strong>begin</strong>. Then you keep doing it <strong>until the “I can’t” quietly turns into “I did.”</strong></p>
<p>That order matters, because most people wait for the proof before they’ll believe — and the proof only comes from doing. You won’t feel capable first and act second; you act, badly, and the capability grows on you like a callus. Confidence isn’t the ticket you need to begin. It’s the souvenir you collect along the way.</p>

<div class="blog-callout blog-callout--tip"><span class="blog-callout__label">The reframe that unsticks people</span><p>Stop waiting to feel ready. “Ready” is a feeling that arrives <em>after</em> you start, never before. Swap “I’ll do it when I’m confident” for “I’ll become confident by doing it.” That single flip is the difference between a plan you admire and a thing you’ve actually built.</p></div>

<h2 id="finish-line-in-your-head">Don’t let the finish line live only in your head</h2>
<p>The most common place a dream fades is not when something goes wrong — it’s in your imagination, fully formed and never begun. You can picture the finished book, the launched business, the fit body, the new skill, in perfect detail. And that vivid picture is dangerous, because it can feel so complete that starting the messy real version seems almost like a step down.</p>
<p>So here is the whole secret, and it’s almost embarrassingly simple: <strong>just begin.</strong> Begin clumsy. Begin slow. Begin embarrassed and underprepared and unsure. None of that matters. The only thing that disqualifies you is not starting, and the only thing that defeats you is stopping. A clumsy first step on the real road beats a flawless one taken only in your mind.</p>

<h2 id="the-real-question">The real question isn’t “when will I get there?”</h2>
<p>When you’re deep in the hard middle, the mind starts asking the wrong question on a loop: <em>when will this finally pay off?</em> It’s the wrong question because it has no answer, and asking it just measures how far you still have to go. There’s a better one to ask instead.</p>

<p>Don’t ask when you’ll reach the finish line. Ask: <strong>have I been patient enough to walk through the failing days?</strong> Being able to step back and see the path is vision. Being able to start walking it is mettle. Walking it all the way through to success — that’s the part the world calls luck or long-term outcome, but it’s mostly just endurance that outlasted everyone else’s. The valley in that diagram is not a sign you’re failing. It <em>is</em> the path. Everyone who arrived walked through it; the only difference is they didn’t turn around at the bottom.</p>

<h2 id="be-more-gritty">How to build a little more grit, practically</h2>
<p>Grit sounds like a personality you’re either born with. It isn’t — it’s a set of small, learnable habits that make “don’t stop” the easy default:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Shrink the step until it’s impossible to skip.</strong> Not “write the book” — write one ugly paragraph. Not “get fit” — put your shoes on. Momentum beats motivation, and tiny steps generate momentum on the worst days.</li>
<li><strong>Show up on a schedule, not on a mood.</strong> Decide <em>when</em> you’ll work before you feel like it. Discipline is just a promise you made to yourself on a clear day, kept on a cloudy one.</li>
<li><strong>Tell the dip from the dead end.</strong> Most “this isn’t working” moments are the valley, not a wall. Before quitting, ask honestly: is this genuinely the wrong road, or just the hard middle of the right one?</li>
<li><strong>Protect the streak.</strong> Don’t break the chain. A bad day where you did the minimum still counts; the goal is to never let a zero day become two.</li>
<li><strong>Make discouragement normal, not a signal.</strong> Expect the failing days. When they come, they’re not proof you should stop — they’re proof you’re on the curve like everyone who made it.</li>
</ul>

<li><strong>The world isn’t short of ideas, it’s short of follow-through.</strong> An average idea executed with grit beats a brilliant one abandoned early.</li>
<li><strong>Three altitudes:</strong> to envision it (vision), to start it (mettle), to keep going until it works (long-term outcome) — almost everyone falls away on the third.</li>
<li><strong>Believe, then do, then do it until you can.</strong> Confidence isn’t the ticket to begin; it’s the souvenir you collect by beginning.</li>
<li><strong>Don’t let the finish line live only in your head.</strong> Begin clumsy, begin slow — just don’t not start, and don’t stop.</li>
<li><strong>Ask the better question:</strong> not “when will I arrive?” but “have I been patient enough to walk through the failing days?”</li>
<li><strong>Grit is built, not born:</strong> shrink the step, show up on schedule, tell the dip from the dead end, protect the streak, and treat discouragement as the path — not a stop sign.</li>
</ul>
</div>
