# Blog Editorial Principles

Source: user request on 2026-06-12.

Use this checklist when creating or reviewing blog content.

- Keep the tone friendly, calm, inspiring, professional, and sincere.
- Keep technical terms in English when translating them would make the article less precise.
- Preserve the current multi-language structure. Any content-facing label or article metadata should work across the existing locales.
- Write for SEO on blog listing pages and article pages: clear titles, summaries, internal links, crawlable images, useful alt text, FAQs where appropriate, and schema data when the page already has a schema path.
- Make articles approachable for non-tech, low-tech, and junior engineers by using familiar examples from office work, teamwork, study, and everyday decisions.
- Add images, diagrams, tables, or practical examples only when they clarify the idea. Do not add generic visuals just to make the article look busy.
- Each article should leave a clear key takeaway that stays with the reader after reading.
- Keep reading-time tracking, views, share, reactions, and the current analytics/event-log behavior intact.
- Use neutral, respectful wording. Avoid sarcasm, shaming, class-based assumptions, regional stereotypes, or any wording that could make readers feel looked down on.
- Do not sell, pitch, or ask readers to inbox directly. Invite readers to share their experience, perspective, or a thoughtful angle instead.
- Prefer a natural, complete article flow. Avoid splitting reflective essays into too many mechanical headings.
- Good hooks come from a small story, observation, lesson, or reflective angle.
- Do not use spiritual framing.
- When discussing meaningful change, explain that visible progress usually comes from quiet accumulation before it becomes obvious.
- Start reflective pieces with a real-life observation, work moment, or small experience before connecting it to career, learning, or personal growth.
- Write with depth, but keep the language easy to follow.
- Do not lecture or shout motivation. Let the lesson arrive through the story and the reasoning.
- Create empathy for readers who have followed the author for a while, and help them see themselves in the situation.
- End with an opening for conversation or shared perspective, not a sales call.
