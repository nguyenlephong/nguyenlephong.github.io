19:T46f5,<p>Two engineers with the same title — "Senior Software Engineer" — can have almost nothing in common about their actual day. One spends the morning aligning four teams on an API contract and waiting on a security review. One ships a feature to production before lunch because she wrote it, reviewed it, and deployed it herself. One is on a call with a client in another time zone, explaining why a change request will cost two extra weeks.</p>
<p>They're not at different skill levels. They're in different <em>environments</em>. And the environment — big corp, startup, or outsourcing — shapes the job far more than the job title does. It decides where your work comes from, how much of it you own, how decisions get made, how you communicate, what "good" means, and what gets you promoted.</p>
<p>This is a field guide to those three worlds, compared along the dimensions that actually change day to day. It won't tell you which is "best" — there is no best, only fit. It will help you read the environment you're in (or about to join), set the right expectations, and operate well in it. The three deep-dives in this series then take each world apart in detail.</p>

<h2>First, what we actually mean</h2>
<p>These are archetypes, not boxes. Most real companies are a blend, and many move between types as they grow. But the three points on the map are clear enough to reason about:</p>
<div class="blog-table-wrap">
<table>
<thead><tr><th width="20%">Type</th><th>What it is</th><th>Who pays, and for what</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>Big corp / enterprise</strong></td><td>A large product or platform organisation — hundreds to thousands of people, many teams, established processes.</td><td>The market pays for the product. You're a specialist inside a large machine optimised for scale and reliability.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Startup (small &amp; mid)</strong></td><td>A young product company chasing product-market fit and growth — from a handful of people to a scaling team of ~150.</td><td>Investors and early customers pay for <em>speed of learning</em>. You're a generalist trading polish for momentum.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Outsourcing / services</strong></td><td>An agency, consultancy, or software house that builds for other companies on contract.</td><td>A client pays for delivery against a scope. You're a professional building someone else's product to an agreement.</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
</div>
<p>The cleanest way to see the difference: a <strong>product company</strong> (big corp or startup) lives and dies by the product it owns; a <strong>services company</strong> (outsourcing) lives and dies by the relationships and contracts it delivers against. Big corp and startup differ mostly by <em>size and stage</em>; services differs by <em>who owns the outcome</em>.</p>

<h2>The dimensions that actually change</h2>
<p>Forget the stereotypes (corp = boring, startup = ping-pong tables, outsourcing = code factory). What genuinely differs between these worlds is a short list of structural things:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Where the work comes from</strong> — how a thing ends up on your plate.</li>
<li><strong>Who decides, and how fast</strong> — autonomy and the cost of a decision.</li>
<li><strong>How heavy the process is</strong> — ceremony, approvals, compliance.</li>
<li><strong>How much you own, and how wide</strong> — specialist depth vs generalist breadth.</li>
<li><strong>How you communicate</strong> — meetings, docs, async, stakeholders.</li>
<li><strong>Pace, risk, and the quality bar</strong> — speed vs stability, debt tolerance.</li>
<li><strong>What gets rewarded</strong> — the behaviours that actually get you promoted.</li>
</ul>
<p>Walk those one at a time and the three worlds come into focus.</p>

<h2>Where the work comes from</h2>
<p>In a <strong>big corp</strong>, work descends through a planning hierarchy. Company strategy becomes org-level objectives (often OKRs), which become a roadmap, which becomes epics for your team, which become the stories you pick up. By the time a ticket reaches you, a lot of people have already decided it matters. The upside: clarity and alignment. The downside: the distance between you and the "why" can be long, and changing direction is like turning a ship.</p>
<p>In a <strong>startup</strong>, work comes from whatever will move the business this month — a founder's conviction, a churned customer, a competitor's launch, a number that's not growing. The roadmap might be a shared doc that changed yesterday. The upside: you're close to the why and can influence it. The downside: priorities whip around, and "urgent" can quietly eat "important".</p>
<p>In <strong>outsourcing</strong>, work comes from the contract. There's a statement of work, a backlog agreed with the client, and a scope that defines what's in and what's a change request. The client owns the <em>what</em>; you own the <em>how</em> and the delivery. The upside: clear boundaries. The downside: you're often building requirements you didn't shape and can't see the full product strategy behind.</p>
<div class="blog-callout blog-callout--note"><span class="blog-callout__label">The tell</span><p>Ask "where did this ticket come from?" In a corp the honest answer traces up an org chart. In a startup it traces to a person or a metric. In outsourcing it traces to a contract and a client stakeholder. That single question predicts most of the rest.</p></div>

<h2>Who decides, and how fast</h2>
<p>Decision-making is where the three worlds feel most different in your gut.</p>
<p><strong>Big corp:</strong> decisions are distributed and protected. Many choices need alignment across teams, sign-off from a lead or architect, and a nod from security, legal, or platform. This isn't (only) bureaucracy — at scale, one team's shortcut is another team's outage. The cost is speed: a decision that takes an afternoon in a startup can take three meetings and a doc here.</p>
<p><strong>Startup:</strong> decisions are cheap and local. You can often decide and ship the same day. Authority is fuzzy and follows trust more than title. The risk is the opposite of the corp's: too little deliberation, so you re-decide the same thing three times and accumulate inconsistency.</p>
<p><strong>Outsourcing:</strong> decisions split along a line. Technical "how" decisions are usually yours; product "what" and scope decisions belong to the client and have to be requested, justified, and sometimes contractually amended. The skill is knowing which side of the line you're on before you act.</p>

<h2>How heavy the process is</h2>
<p>Every team says it "does Agile". What that means in practice scales with the cost of being wrong.</p>
<div class="blog-table-wrap">
<table>
<thead><tr><th width="22%">Process</th><th>Big corp</th><th>Startup</th><th>Outsourcing</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>Ceremony</strong></td><td>Full Scrum/SAFe, multiple layers, formal planning</td><td>Light or improvised; "standup" might be a Slack thread</td><td>Scrum tailored to the client; their cadence often wins</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Approvals</strong></td><td>Many gates: review, security, change advisory</td><td>Few; ship and learn</td><td>Client sign-off on scope, demos, and acceptance</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Documentation</strong></td><td>Heavy and durable (audits, handover, compliance)</td><td>Minimal; the code and the founders' heads <em>are</em> the docs</td><td>Contractual; docs are deliverables you're paid for</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Estimation</strong></td><td>Used for capacity planning across teams</td><td>Often skipped or rough</td><td>Tied to billing — estimates can become commitments</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
</div>
<p>If you want the standard these are all bending — what epics, stories, points, and ceremonies are <em>supposed</em> to be, and how teams customise them — that's the subject of <a href="/blog/ways-of-working/agile-scrum-in-practice">Agile &amp; Scrum in Practice</a>. Here the point is simpler: process weight tracks the cost of a mistake. A bank's payments platform earns its gates. A pre-revenue startup that runs heavy process is mostly performing maturity it can't afford.</p>

<h2>How much you own, and how wide</h2>
<p>This is the dimension that surprises people most when they switch worlds.</p>
<p>In a <strong>big corp</strong>, ownership is <em>deep and narrow</em>. You might own one service, one part of the pipeline, one slice of the UI — and know it cold. There are specialists for the things you don't touch: SREs, DBAs, a design system team, a release team. You go deep, and you lean on others for breadth.</p>
<p>In a <strong>startup</strong>, ownership is <em>wide and shallow-by-necessity</em>. The same week you might write a backend endpoint, fix the CSS, set up a deploy, talk to a customer, and argue about pricing. You own outcomes, not tickets. The thrill is range and impact; the tax is that you're often doing things you're not expert at, on top of a system only a couple of people fully understand.</p>
<p>In <strong>outsourcing</strong>, ownership is <em>defined by the engagement</em>. On a staff-augmentation contract you might slot into a client's team and own a component like one of their engineers. On a project-based contract your team owns delivery end to end — but only until handover, after which someone else inherits it. Either way, you're responsible for the work and the <em>relationship</em>, which is its own form of ownership most product engineers never practise.</p>
<div class="blog-callout blog-callout--tip"><span class="blog-callout__label">A useful reframe</span><p>Don't ask "what will I build?" Ask "<strong>how wide is my circle of ownership, and who's inside it?</strong>" Narrow-and-deep, wide-and-shallow, and bounded-by-contract are three different jobs that happen to share a job title.</p></div>

<h2>How you communicate</h2>
<p><strong>Big corp</strong> runs on written, async, and durable communication because the audience is large and changes over time: design docs, RFCs, tickets that explain themselves, decisions recorded so the next team can find them. You'll spend real time managing <em>stakeholders</em> — people affected by your work who aren't on your team. Meetings are many; the skill is keeping them few and useful.</p>
<p><strong>Startup</strong> runs on high-bandwidth, in-person-or-Slack, low-ceremony communication. You can lean over and ask. The danger is that important context lives only in people's heads and DMs, so when the team doubles, half the knowledge evaporates. The best small teams start writing things down <em>before</em> it hurts.</p>
<p><strong>Outsourcing</strong> adds a second audience you must serve deliberately: the client. Communication is part of the product. Status updates, demos, written confirmations of scope, and clear escalation of risks aren't overhead — they're how trust (and the next contract) is built. Time-zone gaps make this harder and make crisp async writing a core skill, not a nice-to-have.</p>

<h2>Pace, risk, and the quality bar</h2>
<p>"Quality" isn't one universal bar; it's whatever the environment's risk demands.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Big corp:</strong> slow-and-safe. The cost of an outage is enormous, so the bar is high — tests, reviews, rollbacks, observability. You optimise for not breaking what works. Tech debt is real but is managed as a portfolio.</li>
<li><strong>Startup:</strong> fast-and-scrappy. The cost of building the wrong thing perfectly is fatal, so you trade polish for learning. Deliberate, conscious debt is a tool. Undisciplined, unconscious debt is how startups die slowly after they survive.</li>
<li><strong>Outsourcing:</strong> quality is whatever the contract and the client demand — and your reputation rides on hitting it. The trap is the squeeze between a fixed budget and a client who expects perfection; managing that gap is half the job.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What actually gets rewarded</h2>
<p>Every environment promotes the behaviour it needs, and that's the deepest difference of all.</p>

<h2>So which one suits you?</h2>
<p>There's no universal answer, but the fit usually follows what you want <em>right now</em>:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Choose big corp</strong> if you want depth, mentorship, world-scale problems, stability, and a clear ladder — and you can make peace with slower decisions and a longer line to the customer.</li>
<li><strong>Choose a startup</strong> if you want range, ownership, speed, and proximity to the business — and you can tolerate ambiguity, instability, and doing things you're not yet good at.</li>
<li><strong>Choose outsourcing</strong> if you want exposure to many domains and clients, fast skill-broadening, and you enjoy the craft of delivery and client relationships — and you're okay not owning a single product long-term.</li>
</ul>
<p>Stage of career matters too. Early on, the breadth of a startup or the variety of outsourcing can teach you a lot fast; the structured mentorship of a big corp can build deep foundations. Mid-career, the question becomes which kind of impact and growth you're optimising for. None of these is a one-way door — many strong engineers deliberately rotate through all three.</p>

<h2>The hybrids you'll actually meet</h2>
<p>Reality is messier than three clean boxes, and knowing the common blends saves you from surprises:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Scale-ups</strong> — startups past product-market fit (~150–1000 people) feel like both worlds at once: startup speed colliding with new process, often awkwardly.</li>
<li><strong>Big-corp "labs" and internal startups</strong> — pockets of startup pace inside an enterprise, with corp safety nets and corp politics attached.</li>
<li><strong>Product-minded outsourcing</strong> — services firms that build long-term products for clients and operate much like an embedded product team.</li>
<li><strong>Staff augmentation vs project delivery</strong> — two very different outsourcing experiences: joining a client's team as an individual, versus owning a whole project as a vendor team.</li>
</ul>
<p>When you evaluate a company, don't take the label at face value. Ask the questions from each dimension above — where work comes from, who decides, how heavy the process is — and you'll place it on the map yourself, accurately, in about ten minutes.</p>

<div class="blog-takeaways">
<h3 id="takeaways">Key takeaways</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>The environment shapes the job more than the title.</strong> Big corp, startup, and outsourcing turn the same role into three different daily realities.</li>
<li><strong>Big corp = deep &amp; narrow ownership, heavy process, slow-and-safe.</strong> You trade speed and proximity for scale, stability, and a clear ladder.</li>
<li><strong>Startup = wide &amp; shallow ownership, light process, fast-and-scrappy.</strong> You trade stability and polish for range, speed, and closeness to the business.</li>
<li><strong>Outsourcing = ownership bounded by contract, client is a second audience.</strong> Communication and delivery <em>are</em> the product; you build others' products to an agreement.</li>
<li><strong>Process weight tracks the cost of a mistake</strong> — a bank earns its gates; a pre-revenue startup performing heavy process is buying maturity it can't afford.</li>
<li><strong>Fit beats "best".</strong> Choose for the depth, range, or variety you want now — and remember none of these is a one-way door.</li>
</ul>
