14:T99d,<p>It is easy to fall into collecting notes as if the collection itself were the point — clipping, saving, filing away, and feeling productive for it. But a pile of notes you never return to is just a tidier kind of clutter. So don&rsquo;t hoard notes for their own sake.</p>
<figure class="blog-figure blog-photo-panorama">
  <img src="https://nguyenlephong.github.io/dom-pub/icdn/notes/why-taking-notes/forgotten-note-clutter.webp" width="1600" height="900" loading="lazy" decoding="async" alt="A Vietnamese professional sorts blank clippings beside an open notebook and a laptop with an abstract note map." />
  <figcaption>Notes stop being clutter when they return context to the person who will need it later.</figcaption>
</figure>
<p>Notes earn their keep for a simpler, almost humbling reason: a note system helps because it deals with — or at least mitigates — the <strong>forgetful nature</strong> of our brain. Our minds are wonderful at thinking and terrible at storing; what felt vivid and obvious last month quietly dissolves. A good note is a small defense against that erosion.</p>
<p>Seen this way, the practice does two things:</p>
<ul>
<li>It lets you <strong>program your future self</strong> — leaving instructions, context, and hard-won conclusions where the person you will become can actually find them.</li>
<li>It <strong>unloads your past self</strong> into the current and future one — carrying forward what you once understood instead of rediscovering it from scratch. And here the craft matters: the quality and expressiveness of the note system decide how much information is lost in each handover. A rushed, cryptic line loses more of your past self than a clear, well-shaped one. Think of a note you wrote in a hurry years ago that now means nothing to you — that gap is exactly the loss worth designing against.</li>
</ul>
