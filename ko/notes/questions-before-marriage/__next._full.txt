18:T3a93,<p>We're taught that marriage is a matter of the heart — love hard enough and everything will sort itself out. But the most peaceful couples I've met tell a different story: before moving in together, they <strong>sat down and made almost everything clear with each other</strong>. Not out of a lack of romance, but because they grasped a simple truth: <em>most marital conflict doesn't come from being different — it comes from expectations that were never spoken aloud.</em></p>
<p>This article is inspired by a very real list — the <strong>24 items</strong> that one woman who'd gone before (let's call her Nhàn) worked through with her partner before they moved in together. She says that because they aligned clearly from the start, they've barely argued since they met. It sounds clinical, but the longer you sit with it, the truer it feels: <strong>clarity at the beginning is the foundation of peace later on.</strong></p>
<p>This isn't a "test" to grade your partner, nor a cold contract. Think of it as a <em>map for conversation</em> — the terrain two people should walk through together before deciding to commit for life. The decision to marry, in the end, isn't the moment of the ring; it's the quiet confidence that you're both looking in the same direction.</p>

<div class="blog-callout blog-callout--note"><span class="blog-callout__label">Why "before," not "after"</span><p>After the wedding, you discover differences <em>while</em> colliding with them — tired and easily hurt. Before the wedding, you discuss those same things while <em>still calm and still choosing</em>. Same conversation, but timing changes everything. That's why this set is precious: it moves the hard conversation to when it should happen.</p></div>

<h2 id="three-lessons">Three lessons before the list</h2>
<p>Before listing the 24 questions, three reflections worth carrying — distilled by someone who's walked this path:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Marriage needs no drama.</strong> When expectations are made clear from the start, conflict naturally shrinks. Peace isn't the absence of difference; it's knowing where the differences are and having agreed how to live with them.</li>
<li><strong>Put commitments in writing.</strong> Spoken words drift away — months later, no one remembers what they promised. Whatever you agree on, write it down. It sounds comically "professional," but when you need it, revisiting what you both once agreed makes the conversation less emotional.</li>
<li><strong>What isn't agreed yet shouldn't be rushed.</strong> In Nhàn's list, the item "having children" was still un-checked at the time — meaning not yet aligned. And she realized: we're only truly ready for something when our mind is genuinely ready. A blank box is not a problem to hide; it is a reminder that something needs more time.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="how-to-use">How to use this set well</h2>
<p>Before you start, a few principles so the conversation builds connection rather than becoming an interrogation:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Don't do it in one sitting.</strong> 24 items is far too much for one evening. Split it across several relaxed conversations, one theme group at a time.</li>
<li><strong>The goal is to understand, not to win.</strong> There are no right/wrong answers. The aim is to know each other and find common ground — or agree on how to respect the differences.</li>
<li><strong>Honesty matters more than a "good" answer.</strong> A polished but fake answer can become future friction after marriage. If telling the truth is hard, that difficulty is itself a signal worth noting.</li>
<li><strong>Allow "I don't know yet."</strong> Some things need time. Mark them, agree to return — the way "having children" was left open.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="group-1">Group 1 — Understanding each other and yourself</h2>
<p>The foundation of everything: who these two people really are, with both their light and their shadows.</p>
<div class="blog-table-wrap">
<table>
<thead><tr><th width="6%">#</th><th>What to clarify together</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>1</td><td><strong>Your partner's habits</strong> — which are hard to drop, should be dropped, and which they don't want to drop? What can you live with, and what can't you?</td></tr>
<tr><td>2</td><td><strong>Strengths and weaknesses</strong> of both yourself and your partner — seen honestly, neither sugar-coated nor exaggerated.</td></tr>
<tr><td>14</td><td><strong>Medical history</strong> — each person's health and family history. This is trust and responsibility, not something to hide.</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
</div>

<h2 id="group-2">Group 2 — Money: speak plainly to avoid hurt</h2>
<p>Money is a leading cause of marital conflict — not from lacking it, but because two people never aligned their <em>views</em> on it. This is the hardest group to discuss and the most valuable.</p>
<div class="blog-table-wrap">
<table>
<thead><tr><th width="6%">#</th><th>What to clarify together</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>3</td><td><strong>Views on the wedding</strong> — scale, style, and how much "the big day" matters to each of you.</td></tr>
<tr><td>4</td><td><strong>Financial situation &amp; plans</strong> — transparency about income, assets, investments, and <em>debt</em>; spending philosophy; the wedding budget and its funding; how to use gift money; and post-wedding finances: <strong>joint fund or separate funds</strong>?</td></tr>
<tr><td>24</td><td><strong>Personal stash</strong> — is each person allowed a private amount without having to account for it? What level is fine, and what tips into "hiding"?</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
</div>
<div class="blog-callout blog-callout--tip"><span class="blog-callout__label">A tip for the money conversation</span><p>Start by each person describing "what money means to me" — safety, freedom, or status? Understanding <em>why</em> your partner spends or saves the way they do makes common ground easier than arguing line by line. Managing shared money is covered in <a href="/notes/tien-bac-trong-gia-dinh">Money in the Family</a>, and the costs of the wedding day in <a href="/blog/perspectives/wedding-prep-for-grooms">A Groom's Complete Guide to Wedding Preparation</a>.</p></div>

<h2 id="group-3">Group 3 — Children &amp; both families</h2>
<p>This is the group where differences can become the deepest fracture if not aligned early — because it touches the most fundamental values.</p>
<div class="blog-table-wrap">
<table>
<thead><tr><th width="6%">#</th><th>What to clarify together</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>5</td><td><strong>Views on having children</strong> — whether, how many, and when? This is often the hardest item to reach agreement on, and <em>shouldn't be forced</em>: if not aligned, don't rush.</td></tr>
<tr><td>7</td><td><strong>Parenting philosophy</strong> — strict or free, who is the primary caregiver, grandparents' role, schooling, discipline.</td></tr>
<tr><td>8</td><td><strong>Caring for both sets of parents</strong> — living together or apart, financial support, responsibility when aging parents fall ill.</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
</div>

<h2 id="group-4">Group 4 — Career &amp; time</h2>
<p>Marriage doesn't erase the individual. Each of you still has your own dreams, work, and need to be yourself. Balancing these deserves to be spelled out.</p>
<div class="blog-table-wrap">
<table>
<thead><tr><th width="6%">#</th><th>What to clarify together</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>6</td><td><strong>Personal career</strong> — how important it is, how far you want to go, how much time per day/week goes to work, and how you'll face pressure together.</td></tr>
<tr><td>10</td><td><strong>Quality time</strong> for each other, for the children, for family and friends — what counts as "enough" for each of you.</td></tr>
<tr><td>11</td><td><strong>Free time together</strong> — what you typically do on days off, and what you expect of each other.</td></tr>
<tr><td>23</td><td><strong>Alone time (me-time)</strong> — how much private space each person needs to "recharge" without the other feeling abandoned.</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
</div>
<div class="blog-callout blog-callout--note"><span class="blog-callout__label">Loving each other doesn't mean clinging</span><p>One secret of lasting couples is respecting each other's space. "Me-time" isn't a sign of distance — it's how each person keeps the best version of themselves to bring back to the relationship.</p></div>

<h2 id="group-5">Group 5 — Values, beliefs &amp; social ties</h2>
<p>Things that seem small but seep into every day of living together — and are often the source of quiet friction when differences go unspoken.</p>
<div class="blog-table-wrap">
<table>
<thead><tr><th width="6%">#</th><th>What to clarify together</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>9</td><td><strong>Social relationships</strong> — friends, relatives, extended family, colleagues, partners: what boundaries for relationships outside the marriage?</td></tr>
<tr><td>12</td><td><strong>Entertainment &amp; shopping</strong> — hobbies, how much to spend on enjoyment, what's "worth it" to each of you.</td></tr>
<tr><td>13</td><td><strong>Beliefs, funerals, family rituals</strong> — beliefs, rituals, each family's traditions, and how to reconcile them.</td></tr>
<tr><td>16</td><td><strong>Privacy</strong> — phones, social media, personal space: where is the boundary you both find comfortable?</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
</div>

<h2 id="group-6">Group 6 — Intimacy &amp; connection</h2>
<p>An area people are shy to discuss, yet it's the glue of a marriage. Avoiding it from the start often leads to a hard-to-name distance later.</p>
<div class="blog-table-wrap">
<table>
<thead><tr><th width="6%">#</th><th>What to clarify together</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>15</td><td><strong>Views on intimacy</strong> (habits, preferences, desires, compatibility) <strong>and on fidelity</strong> — how you define betrayal, and your expectations of an exclusive bond.</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
</div>

<h2 id="group-7">Group 7 — The rules for running a home</h2>
<p>If the six groups above are "values," this group is "daily operations" — the agreements that keep a home running smoothly without anyone having to guess what the other wants.</p>
<div class="blog-table-wrap">
<table>
<thead><tr><th width="6%">#</th><th>What to clarify together</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>17</td><td><strong>Rules of conduct</strong> — what's encouraged/discouraged, allowed/not allowed in the relationship.</td></tr>
<tr><td>18</td><td><strong>Basis for decisions</strong> — how big choices get made: discussed together, who decides what, on what criteria.</td></tr>
<tr><td>19</td><td><strong>Housework</strong> — how to divide it fairly, who does what, what to outsource.</td></tr>
<tr><td>20</td><td><strong>What's encouraged</strong> — which good habits and gestures the two of you want to nurture for each other.</td></tr>
<tr><td>21</td><td><strong>A "user manual" for each other</strong> — how does each person need to be loved, comforted, encouraged? (Everyone has a different "love language.")</td></tr>
<tr><td>22</td><td><strong>How to resolve conflict</strong> — a "when-heated" agreement: no hurtful words, no walking out mid-argument, revisit when calm. This may be the most valuable agreement on the whole list.</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
</div>
<div class="blog-callout blog-callout--tip"><span class="blog-callout__label">Item #22 deserves priority</span><p>Every couple will fight at some point — what matters isn't "whether you argue" but "whether you're still close afterward." Agreeing in advance on a "when-heated" rule (e.g., don't drag up the past, no harsh words, whoever calms down first makes the first move toward peace) keeps anger from leaving scars.</p></div>

<h2 id="when-to-pause">When to... pause and think more</h2>
<p>This set doesn't only help you move forward — sometimes it helps you realize you need more time, or need to stop. A few signals worth noting:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>You can't be honest with each other about important things.</strong> If merely <em>talking</em> about money, children, or family already becomes tense, that avoidance is itself the answer.</li>
<li><strong>One person consistently dodges, brushes off, or jokes away</strong> every attempt at a serious conversation about the future.</li>
<li><strong>Core differences that can't be reconciled</strong> — for example, one person firmly wants children, the other firmly doesn't. Love isn't always enough to dissolve a foundational value.</li>
<li><strong>You find yourself having to "perform"</strong> to be accepted, instead of being loved as your true self.</li>
</ul>
<div class="blog-callout blog-callout--warn"><span class="blog-callout__label">Saying "not yet" is a brave decision too</span><p>The decision to marry is important enough that the decision <em>not to yet</em> deserves the same respect. Taking more time to be sure isn't a lack of love — it's respecting both of you enough not to step into a lifelong commitment while too many boxes are still blank.</p></div>

<div class="blog-takeaways">
<h3 id="takeaways">Key takeaways</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Marriage is a decision, not just a feeling.</strong> And good decisions are built on clarity, not chance.</li>
<li><strong>Most conflict comes from unspoken expectations.</strong> Clarifying before the wedding is the most effective way to protect the relationship.</li>
<li><strong>24 questions, 7 groups:</strong> understanding each other &amp; yourself · money · children &amp; family · career &amp; time · values &amp; beliefs · intimacy · rules for running a home.</li>
<li><strong>The goal is to understand, not to win.</strong> No right/wrong answers — only common ground and ways to respect the differences.</li>
